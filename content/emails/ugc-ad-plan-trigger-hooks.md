A baby-bottle founder briefed three UGC creators the way most founders do: one script, one hook, sent to everyone. "Love this bottle, no more leaks." Then she waited to see who came back with something usable.

That's not a UGC strategy. It's a hope that one of three people happens to say the right thing in front of a camera.

Here's the problem with a single generic hook: you don't actually know which psychological lever gets a new parent to stop scrolling. Is it permission ("you're allowed to stop feeling guilty about formula")? Is it fear_of_loss (the 2am leak that ruins the sheets, again)? Is it identity (the parent who researches everything before buying anything)? A generic hook picks none of these on purpose — it just picks the safest, blandest version of all of them, which is why it converts like all of them: barely.

`generate_ugc_ad_plan` starts somewhere different. It casts the persona straight from your customer avatar — same vocabulary, same objections, same life stage — instead of a generic "happy customer." Then it writes three spoken hooks over one body script, each angled at a different decision trigger. Same product demo, same claim-gated talking points, three different reasons to keep watching.

For the baby-bottle founder, that meant one creator opened with the leak-at-2am fear, one opened with "I researched twelve bottles before this one" (identity), and one opened with permission to stop overthinking it. Same 30 seconds of footage underneath. Three different audiences hooked at the door.

The action isn't picking your favorite hook and running with it. It's testing all three against your actual audience before you scale spend on any single one. Which trigger the room responds to is data — your gut isn't.
