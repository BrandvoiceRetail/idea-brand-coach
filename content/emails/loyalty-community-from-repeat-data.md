A dog-treat-subscription founder we'll call Sam treated every buyer the same — same welcome flow, same emails, same offers, whether it was someone's first order or their ninth.

That felt fair. It was actually a missed signal.

`get_sequence_performance` broke his email data down by step instead of one blended number, and a segment stood out immediately: customers on their third-plus renewal opened almost everything, clicked things nobody else clicked, and replied to emails that weren't asking for a reply. They weren't behaving like subscribers anymore. They were behaving like people who'd already decided this brand was theirs.

Sam had never built anything for that group specifically. He ran `run_funnel_audit` against his full touchpoint map to check, and `loyalty_community` came back as the clear weakest link — not a thin version of something live, but a canonical position with nothing built behind it at all. He'd built awareness content, a solid listing, a decent welcome series, and stopped there. The customers most ready to go deeper had nowhere to go.

That's the pattern worth noticing: `loyalty_community` doesn't usually get skipped because it's hard to justify. It gets skipped because the proof it's ready sits inside sequence-performance numbers nobody segments by tenure. Everyone can see the total repeat rate. Almost nobody isolates the third-plus-renewal group and asks what that specific group actually wants next.

Sam didn't design a community from a guess about what dog-treat subscribers might enjoy. He built it around the people already behaving like members — the same segment `get_sequence_performance` had already flagged as different from everyone else.

If you've never looked at your own sequence data broken out by renewal number, that's the first place to look before building anything new for your best customers.
