A magnesium-gummy founder watches search CTR sit at 0.3% and calls it the algorithm. Amazon changed something. The category got more competitive. Ad costs are up everywhere, so of course fewer people click.

Maybe. But "the algorithm" is where a diagnosis goes to die, because it's not a cause you can act on.

`get_funnel_piece_metrics` pulled the actual history on her `amazon_main_image`, and the number that mattered wasn't the CTR itself — it was the timestamp. That image hadn't changed in eight months. Meanwhile the two competitors ranking above her had each refreshed their main image twice in that window. Same category, same season, same ad spend pressure. The one variable that moved for them and stood still for her was the image itself.

That's the mini-lesson worth keeping: CTR is not a market-conditions metric, it's an image-freshness metric first. The search grid is a visual competition happening dozens of times a second in someone's thumb-scroll. A static image doesn't get worse on its own — it gets worse relative to everyone else's, which is a completely different problem than "the algorithm changed."

The instinct after seeing a CTR number like that is to raise the ad budget and buy back the clicks you're losing. That's paying to compensate for a stale image instead of fixing the stale image — a permanent tax instead of a one-time fix.
