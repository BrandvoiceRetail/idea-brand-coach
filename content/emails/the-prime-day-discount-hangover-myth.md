A card-game founder watched conversion rate slide the week after Prime Day ended and reached for the obvious story: the discount's gone, so of course fewer people are converting. Case closed, nothing to fix, wait for the next sale.

That explanation is comforting because it means the listing was never the problem. It's also usually wrong, or at least incomplete.

Here's the myth: people convert during a sale because of the discount, and stop converting when the discount stops, so a post-sale CVR dip is just gravity, not a signal. It sounds like basic economics. It isn't the whole picture, because the discount isn't the only thing that changed — the audience changed too.

Run `run_trust_gap` on the same `amazon_listing_copy` before and after the event, scored against who's actually browsing right now, and the pillar scores usually don't move much. The listing is the same listing. What's different is who's reading it. Prime Day traffic skews toward shoppers already primed to buy something in that category that week — deal-hunting, decision already half-made, low bar to clear. The weeks after skew toward colder browsers who need to be convinced from zero, and your bullets were written for the primed version of that shopper, not this one.

That's a listing problem wearing a seasonal costume. The Insight-Driven and Empathetic pillars especially tend to hold the discount-era shopper's shorthand — assuming urgency and category familiarity that a colder browser doesn't have yet.

The action: run `run_trust_gap` on your listing right now, not against last month's shopper, against this week's. If a pillar reads weak for the audience actually landing on the page today, that's your real post-event dip — not the missing discount, a listing calibrated for a buyer who isn't there anymore.

Discounts end. The listing doesn't get to.
