A cast-iron cookware founder sent over her listing asking why CVR had gone flat for three months straight. The bullets were accurate. Wall thickness, pour weight, heat retention numbers down to the ounce.

Read it back to yourself out loud. It sounds like the manual that comes in the box, not something that made you want the pan.

`run_trust_gap` scored her four pillars and Empathetic came back weakest, by a wide margin. Insight-Driven and Authentic were both fine — she clearly knew cast iron. What was missing was any sign she knew the person standing at the stove, nervous about ruining dinner in front of guests.

Here's the mini-lesson: specs describe the product. Felt moments describe the customer's life with the product. A listing can be 100% accurate and still say nothing to anyone, because accuracy and empathy are answering two different questions. "3mm wall thickness" answers "what is this." It never gets near "will I embarrass myself searing a steak tonight."

The fix wasn't a full rewrite. It was moving the felt moment to where it actually gets read. `generate_main_image_title_plan` treats the main image and the title as one positioning statement, not two separate assets — and it front-loads the title's first ~80 characters with the difference that matters, before Amazon truncates the rest. For her, that meant leading with "sears steak restaurant-hot, no smoking, no sticking" instead of the thickness spec, and letting the main image carry the same promise visually instead of just showing the pan on a white background.

Nothing about the product changed. What changed is that the first eighty characters someone reads in the search grid now speak to the moment they're actually worried about, not the moment the factory spec sheet was written for.

If your bullets read like they were written for a warehouse audit, that's usually an Empathetic gap, not a copywriting gap. Run the diagnostic, see which pillar is actually weak before you touch a word.
