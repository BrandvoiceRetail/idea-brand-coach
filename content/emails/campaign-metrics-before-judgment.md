A home-office-chair founder pulled up her ad dashboard last week and told us the campaign was "doing fine" — ROAS was above 2, spend was flat. Then we asked what the campaign was actually supposed to fix. Silence.

That's the gap. The ad platform's dashboard reports what it's built to report: clicks, spend, a blended ROAS across every ad in the account. It doesn't know which funnel position that specific campaign was aimed at, so it can't tell you whether it worked.

If a campaign exists to fix a weak amazon_main_image CTR, "ROAS is fine" doesn't answer the real question — did the new creative move CTR at all? If it exists to rebuild trust after a rough review stretch in paid_social_creative, ROAS tells you even less. A platform-level number always answers a platform-level question, never a diagnosis-level one.

This is what `create_campaign` is for at the start: it ties a campaign to the specific funnel position and problem it's meant to solve, not just a budget and a launch date. Then `get_campaign_metrics` reports back against that same position — so "doing fine" becomes an actual number tied to the thing you were trying to fix, not a vibe borrowed from the platform's own summary screen.

The action this week: before you scale or kill your next campaign, skip the platform dashboard first. Pull it through `get_campaign_metrics` and check the number for the specific funnel position it was built to move. If that number didn't move, the platform's ROAS is telling you something else entirely — usually that other ads in the account are doing the work this one gets credit for.
