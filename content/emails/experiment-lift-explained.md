A beard-oil founder added a trust badge to his listing last month. "It definitely helped," he told us. Conversion felt up. Traffic felt steadier. He was ready to add the same badge to five other listings.

We asked for the number. He didn't have one.

"Felt better" is a real signal — founders who live inside their own data develop good instincts. But it's not a lift number, and trust_badges_social_proof is exactly the kind of change that's easy to feel good about for the wrong reason: a badge landing during a naturally strong week looks identical to a badge that actually worked.

`get_experiment_lift` computes the real delta from the actual test — not the surrounding week's noise, not the founder's gut. It isolates what the badge specifically did to the funnel position it touched, so "helped" becomes a number you can defend, repeat, or walk back.

This matters most for the changes that are cheapest to make and easiest to feel confident about — a badge, a trust seal, a line of social proof near the buy box. Because they're low-effort, founders tend to skip the read and just roll them out everywhere the moment one feels like it worked. That's how a change that did nothing ends up copied across an entire catalog, quietly doing nothing five more times.

The action: before you copy that trust badge onto five more listings, run `get_experiment_lift` on the one test you already ran. If the number backs up the feeling, roll it out with confidence. If it doesn't, you just saved yourself from scaling a change that never earned it — and you can go looking for what actually moved trust_badges_social_proof instead.

Instinct gets you to the test. `get_experiment_lift` tells you whether the test actually won.
