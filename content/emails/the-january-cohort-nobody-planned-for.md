Fatima, a prenatal-vitamin founder we'll call her, had a good Q4. A spike of gift orders — partners and mothers buying a bottle for someone newly pregnant — pushed volume well above her normal trickle.

By January, that spike becomes a problem her existing winback_replenishment sequence wasn't built for.

Her sequence talks to the buyer: "time to reorder, here's your usual bottle." But the buyer isn't who's taking the vitamins. The gift recipient is. She's the one whose bottle is actually running low, and she has no relationship with the brand yet — no account, no email on file that isn't her sister's.

The winback message is aimed at the wrong person entirely.

`create_email_sequence` builds a version aimed at the recipient instead — not "reorder your usual," but "here's what to do now that you're running low on the bottle someone gave you." Different opening, different trust to establish, same underlying replenishment timing.

The timing itself isn't a guess. `get_sequence_performance` on her existing sequences shows when buyers who actually use the product tend to come back — and prenatal timelines don't run on a normal replenishment clock. A recipient who started in October isn't due in the same week a February buyer would be.

The lesson: a gift spike doesn't just mean more orders. It means a second audience with a different relationship to the product, running on a different clock, that your existing sequence was never written for.

If Q4 handed you a gift-buyer spike, don't wait for January silence to notice. Build the recipient-facing sequence now with `create_email_sequence`, and let `get_sequence_performance` tell you when this specific cohort actually comes back.
