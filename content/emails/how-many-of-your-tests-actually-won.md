An ergonomic-office-chair founder ran eight `design_test` experiments against his shopify_pdp this year — a new hero image, a different bullet order, a sizing-chart module, a review-carousel placement, more.

Ask him which ones won and he'll tell you two, maybe three. Confidently. The rest are gone, filed under "we tried something there at some point."

That's not a memory problem. It's what happens when tests don't get a hard verdict at the time. A small bump gets noticed, called a win, and remembered. A flat result gets forgotten because there was nothing to remember. Felt memory keeps the wins and quietly deletes the losses, which means a founder's mental win rate is always better than his actual one.

`get_experiment_lift` pulled across all eight tests gives the real number — not "the ones I remember," every test, scored the same way. For most founders who run this, the honest win rate comes in lower than the felt one. Not because the tests were bad. Because forgotten losses don't count against memory, only against the number.

That gap matters going into next year's test calendar. If three of your last eight wins were real and the mental tally said five, you've been over-trusting your own instinct about what works on this specific page — and you're about to plan next year's tests on that inflated read.

The action: before you build next year's PDP test calendar, run `get_experiment_lift` across everything you tested this year, not just the ones you remember. Then use `design_test` to structure whatever's next off the real number, not the flattering one.
