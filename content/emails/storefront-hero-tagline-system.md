An outdoor-cooler founder we worked with had a Store hero that could have belonged to any brand on Amazon. 3000×600 pixels, top of the page, prime real estate — filled with "20% OFF SELECT COOLERS."

That's not a brand statement. That's a banner ad wearing a brand's storefront.

The hero is the first thing a shopper sees after clicking through from the listing. It's the moment your brand either says something only you could say, or says nothing at all and asks for a sale instead. A discount code isn't distinctive — every competitor has one this week too.

Below the hero, the category tiles usually make the same mistake in miniature: generic labels like "Shop All" or "New Arrivals" that describe the catalog instead of the customer's job. A tile should say what the shopper is *trying to do*, not what section of the warehouse they're browsing.

`generate_storefront_messaging_plan` builds the hero and the tile system together, as one messaging layer instead of two separate design tasks. It pulls a signature-derived tagline — the one line your brand actually owns — and carries that line's logic down into job-mapped category tiles, so "Shop All" becomes something closer to "Pack for a weekend, not just a cooler." The hero states the position; the tiles prove it applies to whatever the shopper is actually there to solve.

The action worth taking today doesn't require the full plan yet. Open your Store and ask: does every tile currently earn its own headline, or could you swap three of them between brands and nobody would notice? If the answer is the latter, the tiles aren't broken — they're just undirected. That's a messaging plan problem, not a design problem, and it's exactly what this tool is built to fix before you touch a single image asset.
