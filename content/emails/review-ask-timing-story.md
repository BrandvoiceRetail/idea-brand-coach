A yoga-block founder we'll call Priya asked for a review in the same email that confirmed shipping. Every order got the ask. Almost nobody answered.

She didn't have a review problem. She had a timing problem.

The shipping-confirmation email arrives before the customer has touched the product. At that point they have no opinion yet — they're not withholding a review, they genuinely don't have one to give. Asking there isn't bad wording. It's the wrong moment in the `review_request_flow` entirely.

`create_email_sequence` builds the ask around when trust has actually had time to form — after the block's been used a few times, after the customer has decided it works the way they hoped. For a yoga block, that's closer to two weeks in than two days. Priya moved her ask to that point in the sequence, and the responses started coming — not because anyone got more persuasive, but because she finally asked someone who had something to say.

The second half of the fix mattered as much as the first. Those new reviews weren't just proof for future shoppers. Priya ran `ingest_evidence` against them and fed the real language back into her avatar — the actual words customers used for what changed in their practice, not the words she assumed they'd use. That's the loop most founders skip: the review flow produces evidence, and the evidence should update the avatar it came from, not sit in a stars-and-count dashboard.

The lesson isn't "ask later." It's ask at the moment the customer's experience actually matches what you're asking them to confirm. Get that timing wrong and even a great product gets silence. Get it right and the review becomes the next input, not just the last output.
