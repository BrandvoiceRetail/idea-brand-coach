A coffee-subscription founder publishes a blog post every week. Good instincts, decent writing, and zero diagnosis behind any of it. Each post is a guess about what her subscriber wants to read, dressed up as content strategy.

Here's a four-question check to run before anything ships, pulled straight from what `create_avatar` and `update_avatar` actually capture:

1. **Real vocabulary.** Would your actual customer use these words, or are they words you'd use describing your customer to someone else?
2. **Real job.** What is this piece actually hired to do for the reader this week? Not "build awareness." Something they could name.
3. **Real trigger.** Which of the six decision levers is this post speaking to: permission, recognition, identity, belonging, momentum, fear_of_loss? If you can't name one, the post is speaking to no one in particular.
4. **Real objection.** What's the specific hesitation this reader has about your category that this piece either answers or ignores?

If a post can't answer all four with something specific, it isn't wrong to publish it. It's just not strategy. It's a guess with good formatting, and guesses dressed as strategy are how a content calendar fills up while seo_content still isn't moving anyone toward a purchase.

The fix isn't slower publishing. It's running the avatar once, properly, so every post after that has real answers to draw from instead of instinct each time. Once `create_avatar` exists, `update_avatar` keeps it honest as you learn more, so the checklist gets easier to answer with each post, not harder.
