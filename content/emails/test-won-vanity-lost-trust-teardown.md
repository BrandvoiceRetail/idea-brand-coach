A diaper-bag founder added a countdown timer to her cart_checkout_flow. Add-to-carts went up 9%. By the usual scoreboard, that's a clean win — ship it, move on.

She'd run it through `design_test` as a proper hypothesis instead of a gut-feel tweak, which meant she also had a before/after read waiting on the other side. When she checked `compute_trust_gap_lift` alongside the conversion number, the Authentic pillar had dropped six points. The timer worked exactly like urgency messaging is supposed to work — and it made the brand feel less honest doing it.

That's the trap with any test that only reports a conversion number. A checkout flow can win on add-to-carts and lose on trust at the same time, especially in a funnel position where shoppers are already primed to feel a little skeptical about being rushed — they've already decided to buy, and now something on screen is telling them to hurry.

Scarcity and urgency tactics aren't automatically dishonest. But they borrow against the Authentic pillar every time they're used, and that loan comes due somewhere — in return requests, in review language, in the next cart that abandons the moment the timer feels manufactured rather than real.

The action: don't call a test a win off one metric. Run it as a structured `design_test`, and check `compute_trust_gap_lift` on the relevant pillar before you scale anything that touches urgency, timers, or scarcity in cart_checkout_flow. A number going up isn't the same as nothing going wrong.

Her timer stayed. But she rewrote the copy around it once she knew what it was quietly costing.
