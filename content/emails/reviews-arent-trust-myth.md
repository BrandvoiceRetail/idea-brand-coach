A pet-supplement founder keeps buying review software. Every quarter, a new tool promising more reviews, faster, with prettier widgets to show them off. Review count climbs. Conversion doesn't.

The instinct isn't crazy. More reviews usually does read as more trust. But "usually" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and her category breaks the pattern.

We ran `identify_decision_trigger` on her funnel. It names the single psychological lever a given purchase turns on: permission, recognition, identity, belonging, momentum, or fear_of_loss. Hers came back permission. Her buyer isn't short on social proof, a supplement for a dog is a small, cheap, low-stakes purchase to feel fine about. What she's short on is a reason to trust a brand she's never heard of with something that goes in her dog's body.

Three hundred five-star reviews from strangers don't grant that kind of permission. One credibility line might: a formulation note from a vet, a manufacturing standard, a sentence explaining why the ingredient list looks the way it does. That's the thing displayed_reviews sits next to on the page, and right now nothing there is doing permission's job.

So before the next review-volume push, try the cheaper move first. Find the one sentence that gives a new customer permission to trust you, and put it where they're already reading, right beside the reviews, not buried in an about page they'll never scroll to. Then watch whether the reviews you already have start converting harder, instead of just accumulating.

Review count was never the diagnosis. It was a proxy for one, and proxies are cheap to chase and easy to mistake for progress.
