A backpack founder we spoke with had a theory about his flat CTR: the main image needed to be nicer. Better lighting. A sharper crop. Maybe a lifestyle angle instead of straight product-on-white.

He got a nicer photo. CTR didn't move.

Here's the myth: the main image doesn't win the click by itself. In the search grid, a shopper sees your thumbnail and your title in the same glance, almost as one object. If the photo promises "rugged outdoor gear" and the first line of the title says "Backpack, 30L, Multiple Pockets," those two things are arguing with each other instead of making one case. A prettier photo making the same disconnected argument just loses more politely.

The mini-lesson: image and title are one positioning statement, not two separate assets you optimize in parallel. The photo earns attention; the title's first ~80 characters (before the fold) have to immediately back up what the photo just implied — real keyword, real point of difference, no filler. When they agree, the click gets easier. When they don't, you're paying for impressions that convert to nothing.

`generate_main_image_title_plan` treats them as one unit. It doesn't just suggest a new photo concept — it pairs the visual direction with the title rewrite and builds a CTR split-test plan so you're testing the combined statement, not guessing at which half moved the number.

The action: read your current title's first 80 characters as if you'd never seen the photo. Does it sound like a description of a different product than the one in your thumbnail? If yes, that's the fix — not the fourth photo reshoot this year.
