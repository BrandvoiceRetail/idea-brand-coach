Alex, a pet-bed founder we'll call him, had one problem: gallery slot four, the size-comparison shot, was confusing rather than clarifying. Everything else on the listing was working. He just wanted that one slot fixed.

He opened `refine_creative_plan`, described the fix, and selected full propagation instead of the surgical single-slot edit.

Propagation isn't the wrong tool — it's the right tool for a different job: a real positioning change that needs to ripple across every live asset at once. Alex had one confusing photo, not a positioning change. But propagation doesn't know the difference between "the whole brand story shifted" and "fix this one thing" — it runs at whatever scope it's told.

What happened next: the size-comparison fix propagated as if it were a positioning signal. Framing language about size and fit started showing up in the amazon_listing_copy bullets, which had nothing to do with gallery slot four. Not chaos — drift. Drift on a listing you didn't mean to touch is still damage.

The lesson isn't "avoid propagation." It's picking the right scope for the job:

- One scene, one slot, one line that's wrong — surgical edit. Scoped, contained, reversible without a second thought.
- An actual positioning change — the brand's real difference shifted, and every asset needs to say the new thing — propagation, deliberately, with eyes open about what it touches.

Alex's fix was real. His tool path wasn't. He caught it fast because he checked the listing right after, which is the habit worth keeping regardless of which path you pick: look at what actually changed, not just at whether the tool ran.

Before your next edit, ask which one you actually have — a slot problem or a positioning problem. They're not the same fix.
