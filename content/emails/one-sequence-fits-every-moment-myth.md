A protein-powder founder runs one flow. The same cadence fires whether someone just abandoned a cart, cooled off after browsing, or is three weeks from running out of a tub they already love.

That's the myth: one sequence, sent at the right time, should cover every reason a customer might need an email. It doesn't, because those aren't the same psychological moment.

Someone who abandoned a cart is deciding whether to trust you at all — they haven't paid yet. Someone cooling off after browsing needs something more patient, less urgent. Someone due for a refill already trusts you; they bought once and liked it enough to keep using it. Send that person the same "here's why you should trust us" copy built for a stranger, and you're answering a question they resolved months ago.

`create_email_sequence` treats these as separate builds for a reason: nurture runs seven steps because cooling a lead takes patience; abandoned-cart runs three because a stalled cart needs speed, not seven touches. Neither template is right for a `winback_replenishment` moment, where the job is closer to a reminder between people with an existing relationship than a pitch to a stranger.

For the protein-powder founder, the fix wasn't writing new copy for the abandoned-cart flow and hoping it doubled as a winback nudge. It was recognizing that "someone's tub is about to run out" is its own psychological moment — closer to a friend checking in than a sales pitch — and building the cadence to match that moment instead of recycling whichever template was already live.

Before you point one more sequence at a new trigger, ask what moment it's actually solving for. If the honest answer is "the same one as last time," it probably isn't the right sequence.
