A hammock founder has five welcome emails. Read them back to back and they say the same thing five times: thanks for buying, here's the brand story, thanks again.

Five emails aren't a sequence if they're all doing one job. A sequence works because each step does a job the others don't.

`add_email_step` is built for exactly this: assigning each step in the flow its own specific role, starting from the first one a customer actually sees, `order_confirmation_email`. That first email isn't the brand-story slot. Its job is narrower — confirm the order, set expectations for shipping, and plant one small trust signal before the box even arrives.

Here's the four-question checklist to run against your own five steps:

- Does step one confirm the order and set expectations, nothing more?
- Does step two show the product in real use, not more brand history?
- Does step three answer the objection that nearly stopped the sale?
- Does step four or five introduce who's behind the brand — once, not repeated?

If two steps answer the same question, one of them is dead weight wearing a different subject line.

The hammock founder's fix wasn't writing new content. It was moving her brand story out of steps two and four, where it had been duplicated twice over, and using `add_email_step` to give each slot one job it actually owns.

Run the checklist against your own five. If more than one step could be deleted without losing anything, you don't have five emails. You have one email, mailed five times.
