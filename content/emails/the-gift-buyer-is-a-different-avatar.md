A phone-case founder built her avatar from the customer she knows best: someone buying a case for their own phone, price-sensitive, cares about drop protection. That avatar is accurate. It's also only half the picture by October.

Q4 traffic skews toward a second buyer entirely — someone buying for a teenager they only sort of understand. They don't know what "MagSafe compatible" means to a 15-year-old. They don't know if the color reads as cool or embarrassing. They're not solving a drop-protection problem; they're solving a "will this be the wrong gift" problem.

That's not a variation on the first avatar. It's a different job, a different vocabulary, a different set of objections — which means it needs its own pass through `build_avatar_stage`, not a footnote added to the existing one.

Run the stage fresh for this buyer specifically. S1 pulls the vocabulary a worried gift-giver actually uses, not the vocabulary of someone who already owns the product. S2 maps the job: proving to themselves, and the teenager, that they picked well. S3 surfaces the trigger, usually recognition — will this read as "they get me" or "they tried." S4 catches the objection that kills the sale silently: "what if they already have a case."

Then run `update_avatar` to keep both avatars live side by side, rather than overwriting the one that's worked all year. You're not replacing your customer picture — you're adding a second one that only matters for a few months.

The place this pays off fastest is `founder_content`. October posts written for the self-purchasing customer talk about durability specs. October posts written for the gift-buyer talk about the moment of handing something over and watching the reaction. Same product, completely different story, and only one of them is speaking to the buyer actually browsing right now.
