A beard-oil founder we'll call Danny had returns copy that read fine to him — because he wrote it thinking about himself as the buyer. Thirty-day window, no questions asked, standard stuff.

Then November traffic showed up, and this year's traffic was mostly gift traffic. The person buying wasn't Danny's typical guy testing a new oil on his own beard. It was his wife, buying six weeks before Christmas, planning to wrap it and hand it over on the 25th — a full month and a half after the return clock had already started ticking.

That's the mismatch. His policy answered a question the guy would ask: "if this doesn't work for my beard, can I send it back?" It never answered the question the gift-buyer was actually holding: "if he doesn't like it, and I only give it to him weeks from now, am I already out of luck?"

`audit_asset` checks a single touchpoint — in this case the shipping_returns_policy copy — against your actual customer avatar, rather than against nothing. Run it against the gifting version of Danny's avatar and it doesn't just say "your policy is fine" or "your policy is bad." It flags the specific gap: the return window is written in calendar days, not opened-gift days, and nowhere does it say what happens for a gift.

Once gifting traffic actually starts landing — not hypothetically, but in the funnel — `run_funnel_audit` confirms whether that gap is costing him anything real. It overlays the avatar across every touchpoint and ranks each one by how well it's landing with the buyer who's actually there this month. For Danny, shipping_returns_policy came back as the weakest link the same week gift search terms started showing up in his traffic mix — not the listing, not the ad creative, the returns page nobody had looked at since launch.

If your returns copy hasn't been checked since before this year's gifting season started, that's worth five minutes before the traffic arrives, not after the refund requests do.
