A home-office desk-mat founder built the product for himself. He works from a kitchen table, hates his wrists aching by 3pm, built the mat to fix his own problem. Naturally, he's sure he knows his customer. He is the customer.

That confidence is exactly the trap.

`build_avatar_stage` runs four stages in sequence: S1 pulls real customer vocabulary, S2 maps the actual job they hired the product for, S3 surfaces the triggers that make them buy, S4 catalogs the objections that stall them. Skip straight past S3 because "I already know" and you skip the stage most likely to surprise you.

For this founder, S3 surfaced a trigger he'd never written a single word toward: a chunk of his buyers weren't remote workers with wrist pain at all — they were parents setting up a kid's homework station who wanted something that looked less "office supply," more "furniture." Same `shopify_pdp` page, same product, a buying reason he'd never have guessed, because it wasn't his own reason.

Being the target customer doesn't make you every target customer. It makes you one data point with unusually high confidence attached — which is the dangerous kind. The founder-as-customer story is a great origin story. It's a terrible substitute for a real S3 trigger map.

Nothing about this means his instinct was wrong. The wrist-pain buyer is real, and the mat genuinely fixes that. The problem was treating one real buyer as the only buyer, and writing the entire `shopify_pdp` page as if everyone landing on it shared his exact ache.

The action: run the full `build_avatar_stage` sequence even when — especially when — you're sure you already know the answer. S3 is built to find the reason you didn't consider, not confirm the one you did.
