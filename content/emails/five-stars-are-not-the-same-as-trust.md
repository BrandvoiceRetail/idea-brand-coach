A freeze-dried-treat founder has a 4.8 rating, dozens of reviews, and a screenshot she's proud of. She also can't scale ad spend without her margin caving in. Those two facts sit together more often than you'd think.

The myth is that a high star rating is the trust metric that matters. It's a lagging indicator of product quality, not proof that your funnel is building trust at the moments that actually decide whether someone buys, rebuys, or clicks past you for a competitor.

Where reviews get asked for matters as much as whether they get asked for. We ran `get_sequence_performance` on her review_request_flow and found the ask firing right after delivery — technically the obvious moment, but not the emotional one. Her product is a treat her dog visibly loves; the peak of trust isn't "package arrived," it's the moment the dog reacts. The ask was landing a full day before the feeling it should be capturing.

That gap doesn't show up in the star rating. High-effort, already-convinced customers leave a review anyway. Everyone else — the bigger group, the ones whose experience would actually move a stranger — stays quiet, because nobody asked them at the moment they felt anything.

`create_email_sequence` rebuilt the flow around that peak instead of the delivery event: a short check-in timed to when the treat is actually in use, then the ask. Same customers, same product, a request that finally lands while the feeling is still there.

A star rating tells a stranger the product is fine. It doesn't tell them your brand understood a moment they haven't lived yet. That second thing is what review_request_flow, done right, actually builds — and it's invisible on the rating badge.

If you're proud of your rating but still fighting for margin, check the timing before you touch the flow.
