An embroidery-kit founder posts three times a week without fail. Ask her which post actually brought someone into the funnel and she'll point at the one with the most likes. That's a guess dressed up as data.

Likes measure who stopped scrolling. They don't measure who clicked through, landed on the listing, or bought. For organic_social_profile, those are two different jobs — and only one of them pays rent.

`ingest_content_performance` closes that gap. It ties your actual posts back to what happened downstream in the funnel, not just what happened in the comments section. Run it and you'll usually find the post you were proudest of isn't the one doing the work — often it's the plainer one, the process video or the honest caption, that's quietly sending people further.

This is the same mistake as judging an Amazon listing by "it reads well." A post can be well-shot, well-captioned, well-liked, and still contribute nothing to organic_social_profile's actual job, which is moving a stranger one step closer to the listing. The metric that matters isn't how the post performed as content — it's what it did once someone left the platform.

The action: stop judging content by likes and run `ingest_content_performance` monthly. Look at which posts correlate with actual funnel movement, not engagement. Then post more like the ones that work, not the ones that felt good to make. Over a few months, a pattern usually shows up — a format, a tone, a topic — that you'd never have spotted from the like count alone.

If you've never checked whether your organic content connects to anything past the like button, that's the thing to fix before you post again this week.
