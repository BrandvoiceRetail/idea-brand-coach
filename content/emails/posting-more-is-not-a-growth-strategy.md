A candle-making-kit founder we'll call Dana tripled her founder-social posting cadence this year. Three posts a week became nine — more reels, more behind-the-scenes, more "here's what I'm working on today."

Her follower count grew. Her follower-to-buyer conversion didn't move. Flat, month after month, regardless of volume.

The instinct when growth stalls is to post more of the same thing, faster. But `identify_decision_trigger` doesn't ask how much content you're making — it asks which single psychological lever this purchase actually turns on: permission, recognition, identity, belonging, momentum, or fear_of_loss. Run it against Dana's actual content and the answer wasn't close. Her posts kept selling belonging — "join our candle community," "you're part of something" — while her buyer's real lever was identity: this is who I am, this is the version of my home I'm building, and this candle is one deliberate piece of that.

Belonging and identity look similar from the outside. Both feel warm. Both photograph well. But a customer deciding on identity isn't looking for a group to join — she's looking for confirmation that this choice reflects her, specifically. Dana's captions kept talking about "us." Her buyer needed to hear about "you."

Nine posts a week of the wrong lever is nine chances a week to miss. Tripling the cadence didn't fix the mismatch. It just repeated it louder, faster, to more people, at the same conversion rate.

That's the trap in `founder_social` generally: it's the easiest place to mistake activity for strategy, because the feedback — likes, views, follower growth — arrives fast and feels like progress even when none of it is buyer-conversion progress.

Run `identify_decision_trigger` before you plan next quarter's content calendar, not after. Fix the lever first. Post as often as you like once the message is actually aimed at the person deciding.
