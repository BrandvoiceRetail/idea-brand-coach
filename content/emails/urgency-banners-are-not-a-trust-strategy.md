A cold-brew founder we'll call Ren added a low-stock banner to her checkout page last month. "Only 6 left in stock." The thinking was standard: manufactured scarcity nudges people to buy now instead of thinking it over.

It didn't move anything. Worse, a couple of customers commented that it felt off — cold brew concentrate isn't a scarce good, and everyone who's shopped Amazon for five minutes knows it.

Here's the myth: urgency messaging is a trust strategy. It isn't. It's a lever, and levers only work when they match what the purchase is actually asking someone to feel.

`run_trust_gap` scored Ren's funnel and found the weakest pillar wasn't Authentic, where a fake-feeling banner would land — it was Insight-Driven. Her copy wasn't proving she understood why people actually buy cold brew concentrate: not for one bag, but to build a morning habit around it.

That's where `identify_decision_trigger` mattered. For a repeat-purchase category like this, the lever most buyers respond to isn't fear_of_loss — missing out on this one bag — it's momentum. The decision isn't "buy now before it's gone." It's "start the routine now instead of putting it off another week."

Once Ren saw that, the fix wasn't a banner at all. It was reframing the urgency_messaging moment around momentum — "start tonight's batch" — anchored to real insight about her buyer's habit, not manufactured scarcity that never fit a shelf-stable product in the first place.

The general rule: before you add urgency copy anywhere in your funnel, run `run_trust_gap` to find the pillar that's actually weak, then `identify_decision_trigger` to find the lever your category turns on. A banner that doesn't match either one won't build trust. It'll just look like every other banner.
