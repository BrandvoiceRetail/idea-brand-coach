A coffee-grinder founder repositioned around durability three months ago. New messaging, new internal deck, new answer whenever someone asked "what makes yours different." Everyone on the team could say the new line without thinking.

The Shopify PDP still opened with "Best value grinder under $80."

Nobody did that on purpose. Positioning changes happen in a meeting, in a founder's head, in a Slack message that says "we're leading with durability now." Assets don't get the memo. They sit exactly where they were written, quietly contradicting the thing the brand supposedly stands for now, on the one page most likely to close the sale.

This is the specific failure mode `refine_creative_plan` exists to fix. It has two paths, and the difference matters: a surgical edit for one scene or slot, and full propagation — a positioning change pushed across every live plan at once. The coffee-grinder founder didn't need to hunt down the PDP, then the A+ content, then the storefront hero, then the email sequence, checking each by hand for the old price-first framing. Full propagation ran the durability positioning through all of it in one pass.

That's the part worth sitting with: the cost of a positioning change isn't the decision. It's the archaeology afterward — finding every place the old story is still buried. Most founders never fully do it. They fix the obvious ones (main image, maybe the title) and miss the PDP, the About page, the welcome email that still opens with a discount pitch instead of the ten-year-warranty story.

The action here isn't "review your assets for consistency," which is the kind of advice nobody acts on because it has no starting point. It's: pull up `refine_creative_plan`, confirm you're choosing propagation because the positioning genuinely changed — not because one slot needs a fix — and let it run against everything live.

If you're not sure your PDP and your actual positioning still agree, that's worth checking before you spend another dollar sending traffic to it.
