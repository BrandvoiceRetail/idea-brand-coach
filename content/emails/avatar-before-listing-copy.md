A baby-carrier founder rewrote her bullets four times this year. New angle each time: comfort, then safety, then price-per-wear, then "as recommended by." Same flat conversion rate after every version.

The pattern is common enough to name. When copy isn't working, the instinct is to write more copy. Try another hook, another round of bullets. What almost never happens is going back before the copy, to check whether the thing being described was ever accurate in the first place.

`build_avatar_stage` exists for that exact moment. It runs four stages in sequence, not as a checklist you skip around: S1 pulls the real vocabulary your customer actually uses, not the words you'd choose for them. S2 maps the job they're hiring the product for. S3 surfaces the trigger that actually moves them to buy. S4 names their live objections. Each stage builds on the one before it, so jumping to S4 without S1 gets you objections written in a vocabulary that doesn't match how your customer actually talks.

She'd been skipping straight to writing, which is another way of saying she'd been guessing at all four stages at once and hoping the guesses were close enough.

Running the full sequence surfaced something her fourth rewrite had missed entirely. Her buyers weren't worried about comfort or safety at all. Their live objection was fit, whether it would work for a smaller frame carrying a bigger baby. None of the four rewrites had touched that once.

Before you write a fifth version of your amazon_listing_copy, run the avatar first, start to finish. The rewrite that follows will be aimed at something real instead of another guess dressed as a hook.
