Take a founder making compostable dish sponges. 4.8 stars, 340 reviews, and a conversion rate stuck at 4% for three months running.

Her first move was the obvious one: stack more proof. A new badge. An "as seen on" line. Another five-star quote pinned near the top.

None of it moved the number.

Here's the lesson worth sitting with: a high star rating and a converting listing answer two different questions. Stars answer "is this legit." Conversion answers "is this for me, right now, with the mess in front of me." Those are separate trust problems, and piling on proof only ever answers the first one.

We ran `run_trust_gap` against her listing copy. It scores all four IDEA pillars, Insight-Driven, Distinctive, Empathetic, Authentic, and names the weakest. She assumed the gap would be Authentic, shoppers questioning whether "compostable" really meant compostable. It wasn't. It was Empathetic.

Every bullet described the sponge: plant-based fiber, biodegradable in 90 days, scratch-free surface. None of it described the moment the sponge actually gets picked up for. Dinner's finished, the pan's crusted, and she wants it clean in one pass, not a materials briefing.

The claim was true and beside the point.

The fix wasn't bullet five. It was the opening line. Instead of leading with the fiber, the rewrite opens on the pan she's dreading right now, then lets the material claim earn its place a beat later, as the reason it works rather than the headline.

Same product, same reviews, same rating. Different first sentence, because the first sentence had been answering a question nobody at that point in the funnel was actually asking.
