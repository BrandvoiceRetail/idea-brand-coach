A fitness-apparel founder told us she'd been putting off video ads for a year because she assumed the first step was a full shot list — scene one, scene two, lighting notes, a shoot day booked with a videographer. That's a real cost and a real delay, so the video ad kept losing to "next quarter."

That assumption is where most founders stall on paid_social_creative, and it's usually wrong for the first test.

`generate_video_storyboard` has two modes, and the one nobody starts with is the one that gets you moving fastest: storyboard-image mode. Instead of planning six or eight individual scenes, the tool composes ONE multi-panel storyboard image — every beat of the ad laid out as panels in a single frame — and that single image becomes ONE video job. No shot list. No crew. No three-week render queue for a first pass.

Per-scene precision mode still exists, and it's the right call once you know which hook and pacing actually work and want tighter control over each cut. But storyboard-image mode is built for exactly the moment this founder was stuck in: you have a hook worth testing and no appetite to book a production before you know if it'll land.

The action today isn't "make a video ad." It's smaller than that. Take the ad concept you've been sitting on — the one you keep saying needs "a proper shoot" — and run it through `generate_video_storyboard` in storyboard-image mode first. See what one image, one job, gets you before you price out anything bigger. If the hook works, you've earned the case for the fuller production. If it doesn't, you've learned that for the cost of one render, not a shoot day.

Reference-kit discipline still applies even in this fast mode — a product sheet from your real photo, consistent character reference if a person's in frame — so what comes back still looks like your product, not a generic stand-in.
