A sleep-gummy founder's UGC script had a creator saying "I finally sleep through the night, every night." Nobody wrote that as a health claim on purpose. It just sounded like something a happy customer would say.

It's still a claim the label doesn't support. "Supports relaxation" and "I sleep through the night every night" are not the same sentence legally, even though they sound like neighbors in a script.

This is the gap `generate_ugc_ad_plan` is built to catch: claim-gated talking points checked against what the product actually substantiates, plus AI-presenter disclosure rails when the "customer" is synthetic. The skeptic-flip line — "I thought this was just marketing, but…" — drifts furthest from the label, because it's written to persuade first and stay accurate second.

The checklist, before anything airs:

- Read every spoken line next to the actual label or listing claims, not your memory of them.
- Flag absolutes: "cures," "eliminates," "every time," "guaranteed."
- Check the skeptic-flip line twice — highest conversion, highest risk, same sentence.
- If the presenter is AI-generated, confirm disclosure language is in the script itself.
- Judge the script as spoken, not as written — tone can turn a compliant line into an implied claim.

The instinct is to review the product page for compliance and assume the ad inherits it. It doesn't. A script is a new set of claims, spoken with more confidence than most label copy uses.

For the sleep-gummy founder, the fix wasn't a weaker ad. It was one word — "supports" instead of "guarantees" — and the hook still worked, because the trigger underneath it was never the false promise. It was permission to stop feeling guilty about needing help sleeping.
