There are exactly six reasons anyone buys anything: permission, recognition, identity, belonging, momentum, or fear of loss. Not sixty. Six.

Most urgency messaging ignores this entirely. "Only 3 left in stock." "Sale ends tonight." These lines get bolted onto listings because they're what every competitor does, not because anyone checked which of the six actual levers this particular buyer responds to. Generic urgency copy is generic because it's aimed at nobody's real trigger — it's a default, not a diagnosis.

Take a men's-grooming founder we've talked with as an example. His urgency messaging ran the usual "limited stock" line, and it did roughly nothing. Not because urgency doesn't work — because his buyer's actual trigger was momentum, the feeling of finally having a routine that's working, not scarcity. The listing was speaking a different language than the one his buyer's brain runs on.

This matters most at the urgency_messaging position specifically, because it's the spot in the funnel where founders most often reach for a stock phrase instead of a diagnosis. It's the easiest place to write on autopilot.

`identify_decision_trigger` names the ONE lever your specific purchase turns on — not a menu of six to sprinkle through your copy, one. Permission sounds like reassurance from an authority. Recognition sounds like being seen. Identity sounds like "people like you." Belonging sounds like community. Momentum sounds like continuing something already working. Fear of loss sounds like scarcity — the one everyone defaults to, correctly or not.

Run it against your product, then rewrite your urgency line around that single trigger. Don't hedge by trying to hit all six — a message aimed at everyone lands with no one.
