A minimalist-jewelry founder wrote her trust-badge copy the week she launched. Two years ago. It's sat untouched since — a small block near the buy box, "hypoallergenic," "ships in 2 days," the kind of text nobody thinks to revisit because nothing about it ever looked broken.

It's about to meet the highest-stakes traffic of her year: Q4 shoppers who don't know her brand, comparing five nearly identical necklaces, deciding fast on trust signals they can only skim once.

`audit_asset` scored that one block cold, against this year's avatar instead of the one she had at launch. The copy still answered questions a first-time buyer in 2024 had. It didn't answer the one this year's shopper actually has, which turned out to be about tarnishing and whether a "gift-ready" claim was true or decorative.

`run_funnel_audit` confirmed it: `trust_badges_social_proof` ranked as the weakest live link, not because it was ever wrong, but because nobody had checked whether it aged.

That's the seasonal risk hiding in anything that "looked fine all year." Launch copy doesn't get worse on its own. The buyer standing in front of it changes, especially the week your traffic mix flips from regulars to strangers deciding under pressure with no history with the brand to fall back on.

A launch-week trust badge was written to reassure a curious early adopter. This year's Q4 shopper is comparing five lookalike listings on a phone and needs a reason to stop scrolling, fast. Those are not the same three lines of copy.
