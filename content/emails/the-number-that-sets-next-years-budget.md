A portable-jump-starter founder we'll call Marcus was about to set next year's ad budget the usual way: last year's number, plus 20%, because that felt reasonable.

He'd run a dozen influencer_ugc campaigns this year — some he'd call wins, some he wasn't sure about, and no consistent way to compare them against each other.

`ingest_content_performance` pulled all twelve campaigns' actual numbers into one place — views, engagement, click-through — instead of the scattered spreadsheet-and-memory version he'd been working from. Then `get_campaign_metrics` turned that raw feed into one comparable figure across every campaign: cost per buyer each creator's content actually moved to purchase.

Not cost per click. Not cost per follower. Cost per buyer who actually crossed the line — because that's what an influencer's content is actually supposed to buy: getting a skeptical stranger to trust the product enough to purchase, not just to watch or tap through.

The result surprised him. His highest-follower-count influencer, the one he'd assumed drove the year's best return, had one of the worst cost-per-buyer figures. A much smaller creator whose UGC leaned hard into an unboxing moment — the jump-starter actually working in a dead-battery panic — had the best.

Vibes said "work with bigger names next year." The number said "find three more creators like the small one."

That's the gap between budgeting on feeling and budgeting on a figure that ties spend directly to the thing you're actually trying to move. Run `ingest_content_performance` on this year's campaigns before you write down next year's number — the comparison only exists once the whole year is in one place, not scattered across a dozen individual campaign reports.
