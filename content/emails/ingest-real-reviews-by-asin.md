Ask a dog-harness founder what their reviews say and you'll get a paraphrase: "people love how sturdy it is." Ask again next month and the paraphrase shifts a little, coloured by whichever three reviews they happened to reread that morning.

That's not evidence. That's a mood.

`ingest_evidence` reads the real reviews — pulled directly by ASIN, not recalled from memory — and turns them into structured evidence: the exact phrases customers use, the specific jobs they hired the product for, the objections that show up more than once. `displayed_reviews` on your listing is a funnel position doing real work; you can't diagnose what's actually happening there off a skim.

For our dog-harness founder, the paraphrase was "sturdy." What `ingest_evidence` surfaced, unprompted, across dozens of reviews, was different: "finally doesn't slip when he lunges at squirrels." That's not a durability claim. That's a specific, recurring moment of relief — and it's a completely different line to put in front of a shopper than "sturdy construction."

This matters because every messaging decision downstream — the listing rewrite, the next avatar update, the ad hook — inherits whatever evidence it was built on. Build it on a paraphrase and you're one skim away from making things up. Build it on `ingest_evidence` output and you're working from what customers actually said, in their own words.

The action: before your next messaging decision — a new bullet, a new hook, a new email line — run `ingest_evidence` against your ASIN first. Don't trust what you remember the reviews saying. Read what they actually say, structured, in one pass.
