Every January, the same impulse shows up: new year, new brand. New logo, new palette, new "elevated" everything.

A resistance-band founder felt it hard this year. Flat growth in Q4, a competitor's slicker rebrand in her feed, and suddenly her own brand looked tired to her — even though nothing about her customer had changed.

Before she touched a single design file, she ran `run_funnel_audit`. It overlays her customer avatar against every live touchpoint and ranks where the connection is actually weakest — not where it feels weakest.

The brand itself came back fine — the audit didn't flag her logo, her voice, or her visual identity as the leak. The problem wasn't the logo. It was her `shopify_pdp` — built eighteen months ago for a customer who'd only ever bought resistance bands from a big-box store, before she'd built any real avatar data on who actually keeps buying month over month.

That's the funnel position doing the damage: not awareness, not the Amazon listing, but the Shopify product page nobody had revisited since launch. Rebranding wouldn't have touched it. A new logo on top of the same disconnected page just makes a prettier version of the same leak.

This is the trap of the January reset. Restlessness gets mistaken for diagnosis. A founder feels stuck and reaches for the biggest, most visible lever — brand identity — when the actual gap is usually smaller, more specific, and less flattering to admit: one page, one email, one line that never got updated.

The fix isn't inspiration. It's inventory. Run the audit first. Let it tell you which touchpoint is actually costing you, then fix that one thing before you open a single mood board.
