A cat-litter-subscription founder can tell you her welcome sequence's overall open rate. She can't tell you which single email in it is actually driving renewals.

That's the problem with judging a sequence by one blended number. Five emails, one open rate, means the strong ones and the dead ones get averaged together — and the average hides both.

`get_sequence_performance` breaks the flow apart step by step: open rate, click rate, and downstream action for each individual email, not the sequence as a whole.

Running it on her own flow surfaced something the blended number never would have shown. Step three, the one she'd assumed was doing the most trust-building work, was barely getting opened. Its job was answering shipping and delivery questions — real content, but content her `support_voice` already covered whenever a customer emailed in directly. The step wasn't failing exactly. It was redundant, quietly duplicating an answer customers could already get elsewhere.

The renewals were actually coming from step four, the one she'd almost cut to shorten the sequence.

The action isn't "delete the worst-performing email and move on." It's reading each step's number on its own, finding the one doing a job your support voice or another touchpoint already handles, and cutting that one specifically — not whichever step just feels newest or least favorite.

A sequence's average can look healthy while one step quietly does nothing. `get_sequence_performance` is how you find out which one, before you cut the wrong step.
