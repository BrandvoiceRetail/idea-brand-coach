A retinol-serum founder sent us her Shopify PDP for a second opinion. It opened with the percentage. Then the pH level. Then a line about encapsulated delivery systems. Technically accurate, clearly formulated by someone who knows chemistry — and completely silent on the one thing every first-time retinol buyer is actually thinking.

That thought is usually some version of: will this make my skin worse before it gets better?

Retinol has a reputation, deserved or not — purging, redness, peeling, a "getting worse" phase that scares off exactly the customers who'd benefit most from starting slow. A page full of percentages doesn't answer that fear. It just proves the product is strong, which for a nervous first-timer reads as a warning, not a selling point.

We ran `run_trust_gap` against the page and it came back exactly where you'd expect: Empathetic was the weak pillar, well behind Insight-Driven and Distinctive. The copy clearly understood the *product*. It hadn't yet met the *person* buying it — someone who needs to be walked through the first two weeks, not handed a spec sheet.

This is the shopify_pdp position doing its actual job wrong. A product page's role isn't to prove expertise; it's to carry someone from "interested" to "confident enough to click buy," and confidence for this buyer runs through reassurance, not chemistry.

The fix wasn't new copy bolted onto the same layout. It was a different structure entirely. `generate_aplus_content_plan` rebuilds the page as one continuous editorial composition — a single visual argument across addressable beats, not a stack of disconnected callouts. For this founder, that meant leading with the "what the first two weeks actually feel like" beat, before a single percentage appeared. The ingredients still show up. They just stopped being the opening argument.

If your PDP reads like a data sheet, that's worth checking before you spend another dollar sending traffic to it.
