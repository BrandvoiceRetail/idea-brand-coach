A stationery-planner founder stopped asking customers to be featured or to leave a review. It felt like begging — asking for a favor she hadn't earned.

That instinct is common, and usually wrong, but not for the reason people assume.

The problem isn't that asking is pushy. It's that most asks are generic — "leave a review," "tag us" — aimed at nobody's actual reason for buying. A generic ask does feel like begging, because there's no relationship in it, just a request.

`identify_decision_trigger` names the specific lever this purchase runs on — permission, recognition, identity, belonging, momentum, or fear_of_loss. For planner and stationery buyers, it's frequently recognition or belonging: they bought into a community of people who care about this the way they do, and being featured is exactly the kind of attention they want.

Run that trigger through the ask, and it flips completely. "Leave a review" becomes an invitation — "we'd love to feature your setup" — and for `ugc_repost_permissions`, the ask stops sounding like a favor to you and starts sounding like a favor to them.

The fix isn't asking less, or apologizing for asking. It's asking around the actual trigger instead of a generic template. Run `identify_decision_trigger` before you write the next ask, and you'll know whether it should sound like an invitation or something else entirely.

Six triggers, one purchase, one lever doing the actual work — the rest is just noise dressed up as marketing copy.
