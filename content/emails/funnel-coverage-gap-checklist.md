Most brand owners can describe their listing in detail and go blank the moment you ask about anything after it. A garage tool-organizer founder had a tight main image, solid bullets, decent A+ content — and had never once looked at what happens between "add to cart" and "order confirmed."

That's not an oversight. It's a blind spot built into how most founders think about their funnel: listing equals the whole job. Everything past it feels like Amazon's problem, not theirs.

It isn't. `list_funnel_inventory` lists every touchpoint you actually have live. `get_funnel_coverage` checks that list against the canonical funnel positions, awareness through advocacy, and shows you exactly which ones are empty. Run both together and you get a real checklist, not a guess: here's what exists, here's what's missing.

For the tool-organizer founder, the first gap wasn't exotic. It was `cart_checkout_flow` — no trust signals at the exact moment a shopper decides whether this specific seller, not just this specific product, is safe to buy from. Amazon owns some of that page, but the brand-controllable pieces — badges, guarantee language, the signals that answer "will this actually ship, and will returns be a nightmare" — were doing nothing.

The checklist:

- Run `list_funnel_inventory`: what do you actually have live right now?
- Run `get_funnel_coverage`: which canonical positions does that inventory NOT cover?
- Take the first gap it surfaces. Not the one that feels most urgent — the first one the tool actually flags.
- Fix that one position before adding anything new anywhere else.

The instinct is always to build forward — a new ad, a new email, a new SKU page. The more valuable move is usually behind you, in a gap nobody's looked at since launch.
