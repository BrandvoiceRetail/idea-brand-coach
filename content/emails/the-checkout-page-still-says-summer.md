A planner-brand founder we'll call Renata built her checkout flow in March and hasn't touched the copy since. Reasonable — checkout isn't the page anyone thinks about once it works. It processes cards, it doesn't need "creative."

Except by November, her checkout page is talking to a different buyer than the one it was written for. March's buyer was Renata's usual customer: self-purchase, no urgency, plenty of time to sort out shipping if something's slow. November's buyer is shopping for someone else, wants gift wrap, and needs to know — right there, at checkout, not three clicks later — whether the order lands before the 25th.

None of that is in the copy. It still reads like a receipt: shipping cost, an estimated delivery range, no gift-wrap option, no line about last order dates for the holidays. It's not wrong. It's just talking to March.

`audit_asset` checks one touchpoint — cart_checkout_flow — against whichever avatar is actually shopping right now, not the one it was originally written for. Run it against Renata's gifting avatar and it doesn't hand back a vague "needs work." It names the missing pieces directly: no gift-wrap prompt, no delivery-by-Christmas cutoff, no reassurance line for someone buying blind for another person.

That's a small, specific fix, not a full checkout rebuild. Which is exactly what `refine_creative_plan` is for: the update path for a surgical edit to one component, not a positioning change that has to propagate everywhere. Renata didn't need a new checkout flow. She needed three lines added and one date made specific, changed in place — without anyone touching the parts that were already working.

If your checkout copy hasn't been looked at since before your last seasonal shift, it's worth five minutes with `audit_asset` before assuming the traffic problem is upstream. Sometimes the ad worked fine. The page just never got the memo that the season changed.
