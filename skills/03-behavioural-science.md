# SKILL 03 — Behavioural Science Foundations

## Purpose
This skill underpins the entire IDEA Framework. It explains the science of why people buy — the psychological and neurological mechanisms that drive purchasing decisions. The app coach must be able to explain these principles clearly and apply them to real brand problems.

---

## The Core Truth: Emotion Drives Decisions

Professor Gerald Zaltman of Harvard University established that up to 95% of purchasing decisions are driven by emotions, not rational thought. These decisions occur in the subconscious mind, and people typically justify them afterwards by creating logical explanations.

This is not a marketing trick — it is how the human brain is wired. Humans are emotional beings who make decisions based on how they feel. Even decisions that appear logical — choosing a laundry detergent, selecting a business software — are ultimately driven by emotional associations.

The implication for brands: you cannot win by out-arguing a customer. You win by out-connecting with them.

---

## Kahneman's Two Systems

Daniel Kahneman's framework from Thinking, Fast and Slow defines two modes of decision-making:

SYSTEM 1 — Fast, automatic, emotional, subconscious. Operates effortlessly. Relies on heuristics (mental shortcuts). Drives most purchasing decisions.

SYSTEM 2 — Slow, deliberate, logical, conscious. Requires effort. Used to justify decisions already made by System 1.

Brand application: most customers decide with System 1 (emotional response to your brand, imagery, and messaging) and then rationalise with System 2 (checking the specs, reading the reviews). This means you must capture the emotional System 1 response FIRST. Features and specifications only matter once trust and emotional connection are established.

---

## The Role of Brain Chemistry in Buying

Emotional triggers in marketing activate specific neurochemical reactions:

DOPAMINE — the reward neurotransmitter. Released when we anticipate or achieve something pleasurable. Creates desire and motivation. Ads or products that evoke hope, exclusivity, or the promise of a better outcome trigger dopamine. This drives the "add to cart" impulse.

OXYTOCIN — the bonding hormone. Released during social bonding. Brands that emphasise belonging, community, or shared values tap into oxytocin. Builds trust and emotional attachment.

CORTISOL — the stress hormone. Spikes during anxiety. Products or messaging that offer relief (easy returns, hassle-free solutions, money-back guarantees) reduce cortisol. Creates calm, confidence, and empowerment.

SEROTONIN — stabilises mood, promotes happiness and contentment. Testimonials, success stories, and rewards programmes trigger serotonin. Creates satisfaction about the purchase decision.

This is the "Shopper's High" — the neurochemical cocktail that makes buying feel good. Brands must create anticipation of how the customer WILL feel once they get what they need or desire.

## Social Identity Theory

Customers don't just buy products — they buy identities. They buy brands that reflect who they are and who they want to be seen as.

Humans are social beings. Much of decision-making is influenced by the desire to belong to specific groups, align with shared values, and signal status and identity. This is why brands like Harley-Davidson, Nike, and Apple create communities, not just customers.

Brand application: your brand should answer the question "how does owning or using this make me feel about myself?" If customers see themselves in your brand — their values, aspirations, and identity reflected back at them — buying becomes effortless.

---

## Heuristics and Cognitive Biases

People use mental shortcuts (heuristics) to simplify decision-making. Key biases that affect purchasing:

SOCIAL PROOF — people follow the behaviour of others, especially in uncertain situations. Reviews, testimonials, "bestseller" labels, and user counts all leverage social proof.

AUTHORITY BIAS — people trust and follow recommendations from experts or authority figures. Credentials, certifications, clinical studies, and expert endorsements build authority.

LOSS AVERSION — people are more motivated to avoid losses than to achieve equivalent gains. "Only 3 left in stock," limited-time offers, and FOMO messaging all leverage loss aversion. Losing something feels twice as bad as gaining something equivalent feels good.

FRAMING EFFECT — the way information is presented significantly impacts perception. "95% effective" is more persuasive than "5% ineffective" even though both are identical.

RECIPROCITY — when someone receives value for free, they feel a subconscious need to reciprocate. Free guides, samples, and value-first content create obligation and increase purchase likelihood.

---

## The Subconscious Search for Emotional Comfort

At the core of all purchasing behaviour is the subconscious drive to feel good, safe, and connected. Shoppers are not just looking for products — they are seeking emotional highs, comfort, and reassurance that aligns with their deeper desires.

Customers are buying outcomes, not products. A face cream is not moisturiser — it is the promise of renewed confidence. A set of toys is not plastic — it is the promise of joy, creativity, and family bonding. A hair supplement is not capsules — it is the hope of feeling attractive and confident again.

As Theodore Levitt observed: "Marketers sell the drill, but customers buy the hole." Always communicate the outcome, not the mechanism.

---

## Implications for the IDEA Framework

- INSIGHT-DRIVEN branding means understanding which emotional triggers and subconscious drivers are most relevant for your specific customer
- DISTINCTIVE branding creates strong memory structures in System 1 — distinctive assets are recognised before logic engages
- EMPATHETIC branding speaks directly to the emotional state the customer is in (before purchase) and the emotional state they desire (after purchase)
- AUTHENTIC branding reduces cortisol by lowering risk and building trust — it is neurologically reassuring

---

## Coaching Application

When a user asks why their product is not converting despite having traffic, the answer is almost always a behavioural science problem — not a product problem. Ask:
- Are you speaking to System 1 (emotion) first, or jumping straight to System 2 (features)?
- Are you creating the right neurochemical anticipation — dopamine (desire), oxytocin (belonging), or cortisol reduction (relief/trust)?
- Are you triggering social proof, authority, and loss aversion appropriately?
- Are you selling the outcome (the hole) or the mechanism (the drill)?
