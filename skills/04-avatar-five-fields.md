# SKILL 04 — Avatar 2.0: The Five Fields

## Purpose
Avatar 2.0 is Trevor Bradford's proprietary customer profiling tool, designed to integrate directly with the IDEA Framework. It replaces outdated demographic segmentation with a forensic, behaviour-driven understanding of customers. This skill powers the Avatar 2.0 section of the app and underpins every Brand Canvas output.

The coach must always start with the customer. Avatar 2.0 is how we do that.

---

## The Shift Avatar 2.0 Makes

Traditional customer avatars rely on broad demographic assumptions — age, gender, income, marital status — leftovers from the mass media era that fail to capture the complexity of modern consumer behaviour. Avatar 2.0 replaces speculation with forensic precision.

It constructs customer profiles based on: observed behaviours, subconscious motivations, and emotional triggers — rather than guesswork. This shifts branding from static segmentation to dynamic, behaviour-driven insights, allowing brands to engage customers as individuals, not stereotypes.

---

## The Five Fields

### FIELD 1 — Buyer Intent (Search)

What it captures: The "why" behind a customer's actions — the purpose driving their search queries, clicks, or shopping behaviour. Not just what they are looking for, but what problem they are trying to solve.

How to gather it: Search terms, Amazon Search Term Reports, Q&A sections, competitor analysis, keyword tools (Helium 10, Jungle Scout), and AI tools like Amazon's Cosmo.

Example: A search for "ergonomic office chair" might reveal not just a functional requirement, but an underlying need to address back pain or improve productivity. The intent is relief and performance — not simply "a chair."

Coaching prompt: "What search terms are your customers using? And more importantly — what problem are they trying to solve when they type those words?"

---

### FIELD 2 — Motivations (Tasks to Achieve by the Product)

What it captures: The deeper, often subconscious reasons a customer seeks a product. Motivations range from the pursuit of convenience to a desire for self-expression, status, or relief.

Most decisions occur in the subconscious, driven by emotional and psychological needs rather than pure logic (Kahneman). Motivations such as social status, convenience, safety, or personal expression often dictate purchasing decisions.

Types of motivation to explore:
- Functional motivation: what does the product need to DO?
- Emotional motivation: how does the customer want to FEEL as a result?
- Social motivation: how does this purchase affect how others see them?
- Identity motivation: does this product reflect or reinforce who they are?

Coaching prompt: "Your customer isn't buying your product. They're buying the outcome. What transformation are they actually paying for?"

---

### FIELD 3 — Emotional Triggers

What it captures: The stimuli that resonate with a customer's feelings and subconscious desires. Emotional triggers are the bridge between what the product does and why the customer cares.

Emotion outweighs logic in most purchasing decisions (Lindstrom, Buyology). 95% of decisions are emotional (Zaltman). The key triggers to identify for any customer:

- HOPE — the promise of a better future or a better version of themselves
- BELONGING — feeling part of a community that shares their values
- VALIDATION — feeling seen, understood, and respected
- TRUST — confidence that the product will deliver and the brand is honest
- RELIEF — the lifting of a burden, solving a frustrating problem
- ASPIRATION — aligning the purchase with their ideal self
- EMPOWERMENT — feeling in control and capable

Additional triggers: Fear of loss, curiosity, nostalgia, status, instant gratification, reciprocity, surprise and delight, fun and entertainment.

Coaching prompt: "Which emotional trigger is most powerful for your specific customer? Are you communicating hope, relief, belonging, or something else — and is that the right trigger for this audience?"

---

### FIELD 4 — Shopper Type 1–4

What it captures: Behavioural segmentation based on Shopify's 2024 State of Commerce Report (survey of 7,628 consumers across Europe). The four shopper types define different purchasing priorities and brand relationship expectations.

THE FOUR SHOPPER TYPES:

TYPE 1 — COST SENSITIVE CONSUMERS (50% of all shoppers)
- Focused on price and value above all else
- Not brand loyal — will switch for a better price
- Likely in semi-skilled or clerical roles
- More pessimistic about the economy
- Strategy: demonstrate exceptional value, use loss aversion, create urgency with promotions. If your product is premium, ensure the value gap is crystal clear.

TYPE 2 — QUALITY FOCUSED CONSUMERS (39% of all shoppers)
- Care primarily about quality over price
- Prefer quality products that last
- Work in professional or higher technical roles
- Will shop online and in-store equally
- Strategy: lead with quality signals, certifications, ingredients, craftsmanship, and longevity. These customers will pay more if you justify it.

TYPE 3 — CONSCIOUS CONSUMERS (9% of all shoppers)
- Prioritise sustainable products and ethical practices
- Willing to pay a premium for environmental responsibility
- Under 34, working in professional roles
- Have maintained or increased discretionary spending
- Strategy: authentic sustainability claims, transparency in sourcing, values-led storytelling. Only 36% would switch for a better price — the lowest of all types.

TYPE 4 — CONNECTED CONSUMERS (2% of all shoppers — but growing fast)
- Don't hesitate to connect with brands online or purchase via social media
- Prefer online shopping
- Under 34, likely male
- Believe brands should offer an integrated shopping experience
- Strategy: social commerce, community-building, seamless multi-channel experiences, digital-native brand voice.

IMPORTANT COACHING NOTE: Most brands have a primary shopper type. Identify it early and ensure every brand decision — from pricing strategy to messaging tone — serves that type's specific motivations and emotional triggers.

---

### FIELD 5 — Relevant Demographics

What it captures: Contextual data points that complement behavioural insights. Avatar 2.0 does not use demographics as the starting point — they are used as context to refine the behavioural picture.

Generational traits highlight how shared experiences shape values and preferences:
- MILLENNIALS (born 1981–1996): purpose-driven brands, sustainability, authenticity, experiences over things
- GEN Z (born 1997–2012): inclusivity, social justice, genuine UGC, anti-corporate, digital-native
- GEN X (born 1965–1980): quality, reliability, value for money, sceptical of hype
- BABY BOOMERS (born 1946–1964): trust and credibility, expert authority, clear product information

Demographics inform HOW to communicate, not WHETHER to connect emotionally. The emotional triggers are the same across generations — hope, trust, belonging — but the language, channels, and cultural references differ.

## How the Five Fields Feed the Brand Canvas

Avatar 2.0 is the mandatory first step before completing the Brand Canvas:
- Field 1 (Buyer Intent) informs Positioning Statement and Value Proposition
- Field 2 (Motivations) informs Brand Purpose and Brand Mission
- Field 3 (Emotional Triggers) informs Brand Voice, Personality, and all messaging
- Field 4 (Shopper Type) informs Brand Values and Target Audience definition
- Field 5 (Demographics) informs channel strategy, language, and tone

Without completing Avatar 2.0 first, the Brand Canvas outputs will be generic and disconnected from the customer.

---

## Coaching Application

When a user says their messaging is not converting, or they don't know who they are talking to, start here:

1. Ask about buyer intent: "What are your customers actually searching for?"
2. Dig into motivation: "What are they hoping to achieve — what transformation are they buying?"
3. Identify emotional triggers: "What emotion drives their purchase — hope, relief, trust, status?"
4. Determine shopper type: "Are they primarily cost-sensitive, quality-focused, conscious, or connected?"
5. Add demographic context: "What generation and life stage does your core customer occupy?"

Then synthesise: "Based on what you've told me, your customer is a [shopper type] who is primarily motivated by [motivation] and emotionally triggered by [trigger]. Every piece of brand communication should speak directly to that."
