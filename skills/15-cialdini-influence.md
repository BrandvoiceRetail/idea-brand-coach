# SKILL 15 — Cialdini: Influence & Persuasion

## Source
Robert Cialdini — Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion (1984, revised 2007)
Application to IDEA: The six (now seven) universal principles of persuasion that drive human compliance and purchasing behaviour.

---

## Core Thesis
Human beings use automatic, heuristic-based responses to make decisions about whether to comply with a request or make a purchase. These responses are triggered by specific stimuli — the six principles of influence. Understanding and applying these ethically is the foundation of effective brand communication and conversion optimisation.

---

## The Six (Seven) Principles

### PRINCIPLE 1 — RECIPROCITY
The principle: People feel obligated to return favours. When we receive something of value, we feel a subconscious debt that we want to repay.

Brand applications:
- Free samples, free guides, free consultations
- Value-first content (educational blog posts, how-to videos, free tools)
- Unexpected gifts with purchases (free gift with every order)
- Generous customer service that goes beyond expectation

Example: When a brand provides a free guide, a valuable email series, or an unexpectedly generous return policy, customers feel a pull toward reciprocating through purchase, loyalty, or advocacy.

Listing application: "Free bonus guide included with every purchase" or "Try a free sample first" directly triggers reciprocity.

---

### PRINCIPLE 2 — COMMITMENT AND CONSISTENCY
The principle: People have a deep desire to be consistent with things they have previously said or done. Once someone commits to a position or takes a small action, they are more likely to take larger actions consistent with that commitment.

Brand applications:
- Ask customers to take small first steps (sign up for a newsletter, take a quiz, follow on social media) before asking for a purchase
- Use "I want to..." framing in surveys and opt-ins
- Subscription models leverage commitment — once subscribed, consistency bias keeps customers
- Encourage customers to publicly share their purchase (social proof + consistency)

Example: A customer who takes a brand quiz and receives a personalised recommendation has committed to the process. They are now more likely to purchase the recommended product.

---

### PRINCIPLE 3 — SOCIAL PROOF
The principle: When uncertain about what to do, people look to the behaviour of others. The more people who are doing something, the more correct that behaviour appears.

Brand applications:
- Review count and star rating (displayed prominently)
- "Bestseller" and "Most Popular" labels
- "X people are looking at this right now"
- User-generated content (real customers using the product)
- Media mentions and "As seen in..." badges
- Influencer usage (authentic, not forced)

Example: "Join over 500,000 happy customers" is social proof. A product with 4.8 stars from 12,000 reviews is social proof. A real customer video review is social proof.

UGC is the most powerful form of social proof in 2026 because: 86% of consumers trust brands showcasing authentic UGC, while only 12% trust influencer-promoted products (EnTribe).

---

### PRINCIPLE 4 — AUTHORITY
The principle: People are more likely to follow the recommendations of credible experts. Titles, credentials, expertise, and demonstrations of knowledge all trigger authority compliance.

Brand applications:
- Professional credentials and certifications
- Clinical studies and third-party testing
- Expert endorsements (doctors, scientists, industry specialists)
- Media coverage from credible publications
- Data and statistics that demonstrate expertise
- The founder's story and professional background
- "As recommended by..."

Example: "Clinically tested, third-party verified, and doctor-approved" activates authority. A supplement brand citing the research behind its formulation activates authority. A beauty brand with a dermatologist-developed formula activates authority.

Agentic commerce note: AI agents heavily favour content from authoritative sources (licensed publishers, institutional citations, high-DA affiliate sites). Authority signals in earned media directly increase AI recommendation likelihood.

---

### PRINCIPLE 5 — LIKING
The principle: People are more likely to say yes to people and brands they like. Liking is triggered by similarity, familiarity, physical attractiveness, and association with positive things.

Brand applications:
- Authentic brand personality that customers find relatable
- Founder story and human face behind the brand
- Shared values (we believe what you believe)
- Community building
- UGC featuring real, relatable customers
- Warm, conversational brand voice
- Associating the brand with positive experiences and aspirational identities

Example: Glossier built its brand on liking — real customers, relatable founder, community over corporate. Customers like Glossier because it feels like a friend who happens to make great beauty products.

IDEA connection: Authentically Human Branding is fundamentally a Liking strategy. The more human, relatable, and values-aligned a brand is, the more customers like it — and the more they buy.

---

### PRINCIPLE 6 — SCARCITY
The principle: People value things more when they are rare or becoming unavailable. The fear of missing out is a powerful motivator.

Brand applications:
- "Only X left in stock"
- Limited-edition products and seasonal releases
- Time-limited offers and countdown timers
- Exclusive access (waitlists, founding member pricing)
- "Last chance" messaging

Example: Supreme has built an entire brand strategy around scarcity through drop culture. Limited-edition product releases that sell out in minutes create intense demand, FOMO, and perceived value.

Warning: Scarcity must be genuine or it destroys trust. Fake countdown timers that reset, false "only 3 left" claims, and manufactured urgency are detected by customers and permanently damage credibility.

---

### PRINCIPLE 7 — UNITY (added in Pre-Suasion)
The principle: People are more likely to comply with requests from those they see as part of their "we" — their group, tribe, or family. Shared identity is more powerful than any argument.

Brand applications:
- Community building ("We are all in this together")
- Values-based identity ("For people who believe in X")
- Exclusive membership models
- Co-creation with customers
- "Brands like us..." framing

Example: Patagonia does not sell to customers — it invites people who share its environmental values to join a movement. The Unity principle is why brand community is so powerful.

---

## Applying All Six Principles in a Single Amazon Listing

RECIPROCITY: Free bonus guide, generous returns policy
COMMITMENT: Quiz or personalised recommendation path before purchase
SOCIAL PROOF: 4.8 stars, 8,000 reviews, "bestseller" badge, UGC images
AUTHORITY: Clinical study backing, dermatologist-developed, doctor-recommended
LIKING: Founder story in brand content, warm conversational copy, relatable imagery
SCARCITY: "Stock limited due to high demand" (genuine), limited edition colourway
UNITY: "For people who believe quality should be transparent" — identity alignment

## Coaching Application

When a user's conversion rate is low despite good traffic and product quality, audit through the six principles:

1. Reciprocity: Are you giving value before asking for the sale?
2. Social Proof: Is your review count prominent? Are you using UGC?
3. Authority: Are you demonstrating credibility through third-party validation?
4. Liking: Is your brand voice warm and relatable? Do customers feel you share their values?
5. Scarcity: Are you creating legitimate urgency?
6. Commitment: Are you guiding customers through small commitment steps before asking for a purchase?

Almost every conversion problem maps to one of these six principles. Identify the weakest and address it first.
