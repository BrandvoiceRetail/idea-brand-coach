CHAPTER 01

THE EVOLVING
ECOMMERCE LANDSCAPE

In a world where trust in corporations and institutions has seriously eroded,
today’s consumers crave more than transactional relationships with brands;
they want trust, empathy, and meaningful connections. The rise of consumer
skepticism, driven by scandals, data breaches, fake reviews and the impersonal
nature of AI-driven interactions, has made authenticity the cornerstone of
successful branding. This chapter explores why this shift matters and how
brands can rebuild trust in an age of mistrust.

Mistrust as the Default State
Consumers today face a deluge of advertising, fake reviews, and misinformation.
As a result, cynicism has become the default state. Research shows that trust in
traditional institutions, including corporations, has plummeted in recent years.
Scandals surrounding data privacy and transparency have only deepened this
distrust.
The erosion of trust in technology platforms has become a critical issue as users
grow increasingly skeptical of their practices. High-profile data breaches, such
as Facebook’s Cambridge Analytica scandal, where user data was exploited for
political purposes, have exposed vulnerabilities in how platforms handle privacy.
To address these concerns, Meta implemented new privacy tools and promoted
campaigns emphasizing user control over data. However, skepticism persisted.
In January 2025, Meta announced the discontinuation of its third-party factchecking program across Facebook, Instagram, and Threads. Replacing it with
the “Community Notes” system, the company aimed to reduce censorship
and empower users to add context to posts. While the move was intended to
foster trust and transparency, it raised concerns about the potential spread of
misinformation. Critics questioned whether reliance on user-generated notes
would weaken the integrity of content moderation.
Meta’s shift reflects broader trends in corporate strategies to balance free
expression and responsible platform management. Whether this approach will
succeed in rebuilding trust remains uncertain, but it underscores the importance
of addressing consumer perceptions of bias and manipulation.
20

Similarly, concerns about misinformation have risen with platforms like Twitter
(now X) and YouTube, where algorithms sometimes prioritize sensational or
polarizing content over accurate information. TikTok has faced scrutiny over
data-sharing practices with China, amplifying fears about national security
and surveillance and possibly may not continue to operate in the USA.
Additionally, the rise of generative AI tools has sparked worries about
deepfakes and the potential misuse of synthetic media to manipulate
public opinion. This growing distrust reflects a broader sentiment that
many platforms prioritize profits and engagement metrics over user safety,
transparency, and ethical responsibility, challenging their ability to maintain
credibility in the eyes of the public.

The Shift in Consumer Trust
Trust is the most valuable asset in business, yet many brands today have made
a dangerous miscalculation: they have outsourced trust to influencers, hoping
for a shortcut to credibility. Instead of building authentic relationships with their
customers, they lean on the borrowed authority of social media personalities,
expecting quick wins. But trust, when rented instead of earned, is fragile. And
when it collapses, it takes the brand down with it.
In this chapter, we’ll explore why brands have abdicated trust to influencers,
why this is an unsustainable strategy, and what businesses must do to reclaim
authentic consumer trust before it’s too late.

The Rise of Borrowed Trust
Influencer marketing took off because it solved a fundamental problem: how
do brands reach skeptical, ad-averse consumers? Traditional advertising had lost
its grip on attention, and consumers turned to peer recommendations and usergenerated content (UGC) instead.
Influencers became the perfect bridge—trusted voices with loyal followings,
making brand promotions feel organic rather than commercial.
21

At first, the partnership made sense. Influencers were seen as relatable,
trustworthy individuals rather than corporate spokespeople. But then, as brands
poured billions into influencer sponsorships, something changed.
Authenticity gave way to transactionality. Influencers became walking
billboards, promoting anything for a paycheck. Audiences noticed the shift. A
fitness influencer recommending weight-loss supplements one week and fast
food the next. A sustainability advocate suddenly pushing fast fashion. The
illusion of authenticity began to crumble.

Why Borrowed Trust Is a Risky Strategy
While influencer marketing still plays a role, brands that depend on it as their
primary trust-building tool are making a critical mistake. Here’s why:
z Trust Can’t Be Transferred
Trust doesn’t work like a hand-me-down jacket—it doesn’t automatically
pass from an influencer to a brand. Consumers might trust an influencer, but
that trust doesn’t extend to every product they promote.
The real relationship exists between the influencer and their audience—not
between the audience and the brand. When the partnership ends, so does
the connection. If the brand hasn’t established its own credibility, it remains
invisible once the influencer stops talking about it.
z Influencer Marketing vs. User-Generated Content (UGC): Understanding the
Shift Toward Authenticity
Influencer marketing and user-generated content (UGC) may seem similar
on the surface—both involve individuals outside the brand promoting a
product—but the key difference lies in authenticity and trust.

22

The Shift from Influencer Marketing to User-Generated Content
The way consumers engage with brands is changing. While influencer
marketing once dominated digital advertising, a growing number of
consumers—particularly Gen Z—are becoming skeptical of sponsored
endorsements. Instead, they trust authentic, user-generated content (UGC) from
real customers. A recent study by EnTribe highlights this shift:
 86% of consumers trust brands showcasing authentic UGC.
 Only 12% trust influencer-promoted products.
 81% of customers believe influencers have little to no positive impact on
their perception of a brand.
 90% trust recommendations from friends or family over influencers.
This reflects a broader trend: “influencer fatigue.” Social media personalities
often present highly curated, polished content that feels disconnected from
real-life consumer experiences. Many audiences now see these endorsements as
transactional rather than genuine, diminishing their credibility.

Why UGC Is Winning Trust
Unlike influencer marketing, which relies on paid partnerships, UGC consists
of organic, customer-created content such as reviews, testimonials, unboxing
videos, and social media posts. It is unscripted, largely unpaid, and driven by real
users sharing their honest experiences. Because of its authenticity, UGC has a
strong impact on consumer behavior:
 82% of consumers are more likely to buy from brands incorporating UGC in
their marketing.
 Seeing real customers use and enjoy a product triggers social proof,
reinforcing trust and purchase intent.

23

The Industry Shift: Building Authentic Connections
Today’s consumers crave honesty and transparency. Brands that prioritize
authenticity—whether by featuring real customer stories, responding sincerely
to feedback, or being open about how their products are made—are better
positioned to earn trust.
As a result, brands are moving away from influencer-heavy strategies
and investing in community-driven content. Instead of relying on polished
sponsorships, they are leveraging loyal customers, micro-communities, and real
experiences to create more meaningful connections.
For businesses looking to stand out in an increasingly skeptical market,
authenticity is the new currency—and UGC is proving to be the most effective
way to earn it.
z Lack of Control Over the Narrative
When brands hand over their storytelling to influencers, they lose control
over how their message is delivered.
Influencers have their own style, tone, and priorities, which may not always
align with the brand’s core values. Even worse, if an influencer is caught in
controversy or their credibility collapses, brands that align with them suffer
collateral damage.
z Short-Term Gains, Long-Term Dependence
Influencer marketing is great for short bursts of visibility, but it rarely
translates into long-term customer loyalty. A brand that relies too heavily on
influencers will always be at the mercy of who is talking about them, rather
than establishing a stable, independent reputation.

24

Relying on Influencers Creates a Cycle of Dependency:
 No influencer promotion? Sales drop.
 Want visibility? Pay an influencer again.
 Repeat endlessly.
This isn’t trust-building—it’s a paid traffic model that forces brands to keep
spending to stay relevant.

The TikTok Dilemma: Building
on Borrowed Ground
Few platforms have reshaped marketing as dramatically as TikTok. Its ability to
turn unknown brands into overnight sensations has made it an essential tool for
businesses. A single viral video can sell out a product in hours.
Brands have become addicted to TikTok, using influencers and organic content
to gain traction. But here’s the danger: TikTok is built on borrowed real estate.
And right now, that real estate is under threat.
At the time of writing, TikTok has been granted a 90-day reprieve from a
potential ban in the U.S., following ongoing concerns about its ownership and
data security. If political pressure forces TikTok’s removal—or if restrictive
regulations cripple its ability to operate—businesses that rely solely on TikTok
will be left stranded.
This isn’t just a TikTok issue—it’s the same Amazon trap many brands fall into.
Sellers who build their entire business on Amazon know they are always at the
mercy of the platform’s rules, fees, and algorithmic changes. A single policy
update, suspension, or shift in search rankings can wipe out years of work
overnight.

25

If brands want to stop renting trust and start building it, they must take control
of their own credibility. Here’s how:
z Be Authentically Human
Consumers trust people—not corporations. Brands that succeed in today’s
landscape are those that act like real people rather than faceless entities.
This means:
 Speaking like a human, not a PR machine.
 Showing vulnerability and transparency.
 Engaging in real conversations with customers.

z Own Your Story
Brands must develop their own narrative instead of relying on influencers
to tell it for them. Storytelling builds trust because it connects on a human
level.
 Why does your brand exist?
 What values do you stand for?
 How do you make customers’ lives better?
When customers feel connected to your brand’s purpose, they don’t need an
influencer to convince them to buy—they choose you because they believe in
you.
z Build Direct Relationships with Customers
The most trustworthy brands interact directly with their customers without a
middleman. This means:
 Investing in community engagement—social media, brand-led forums,
live Q&As.
26

 Encouraging user-generated content—real testimonials and organic
brand advocacy.
 Rewarding loyalty and repeat customers—exclusive perks for those who
engage consistently.

z Create a Multi-Channel Strategy
No brand should be at the mercy of one platform. Whether it’s TikTok,
Amazon, or Instagram, diversification is key. Brands should:
 Develop owned marketing channels like email lists and SMS campaigns.
 Invest in their own website and direct-to-consumer sales.
 Engage with customers across multiple platforms, ensuring they aren’t
reliant on a single traffic source.

The Bottom Line: Don’t Rent Trust—Own It
TikTok might survive this political battle, or it might not. Either way, brands
should take this as a wake-up call. The same way Amazon sellers constantly face
suspensions, fee hikes, and lost visibility, TikTok-dependent businesses now face
the reality of impermanence.
No brand should be at the mercy of an algorithm, a policy change, or a
government decision. Building your own audience, your own community, and
your own multi- channel trust strategy is the only way to future-proof your
business.
The lesson is clear: stop renting trust—own it. The brands that take control now
will be the ones still standing, no matter what happens to TikTok, Amazon, or
any other third-party platform.

Amazon - Far from an Even Playing Field
Can Amazon balance trust and fairness? Amazon’s dominance in e-commerce
is built on a foundation of trust, driven by its relentless focus on the customer
experience. With unparalleled convenience, reliability, and personalization,
Amazon has become the go-to marketplace for millions of shoppers worldwide.
This trust has also attracted sellers, offering them access to a global audience.
However, this opportunity comes with challenges, as sellers must operate within
Amazon’s strict rules and navigate fierce competition.
One of the critical areas where Amazon has taken a proactive stance is ensuring
product safety and compliance, particularly in light of rising concerns about
non-compliant imports. Recent inspections in the European Union revealed
that 85–95% of products from platforms like Shein, AliExpress, and Temu fail
to meet EU product legislation. These products often pose serious risks, from
choking hazards to overheating electronics to banned substances. With over one
billion e-commerce parcels entering the EU annually through the Netherlands,

playing classic Nintendo games like Super Mario or The Legend of Zelda,

fond childhood memories. This nostalgia drives emotional engagement,
making fans excited to relive their past while introducing the brand to a new
generation.
z Achievement and Status:
Many consumers seek recognition and validation, and brands that

These emotional triggers are powerful tools that brands can use to build

The Balance Between Logic and Emotion
While this chapter has focused primarily on the power of emotion in building
trust, it’s important to recognize that a successful brand doesn’t ignore logic
entirely. Effective branding strikes a balance between emotional appeal and
rational value. Customers may be drawn to a brand because of how it makes
them feel, but they still need logical reasons to justify their purchase—such as
product quality, price, or functionality, even if this is post rationalization.
This balance is key because emotions may spark interest or loyalty, but rational
justifications can lead to sustained buying behavior. For instance, while Apple
evokes strong emotions of creativity and innovation, the company also backs
up these feelings with logical value: high-quality hardware, an integrated
ecosystem of products, and excellent customer support. It’s the combination of
these emotional and rational factors that makes the brand so powerful.

Difference vs. Distinctiveness:
The Keys to Brand Success
In branding and marketing, “difference” and “distinctiveness” serve two separate
but complementary purposes. Understanding their roles can help businesses
position themselves effectively in the marketplace.

Difference: What Makes Your Product or Service Unique
Difference refers to the tangible or functional aspects that set a product or
service apart from its competitors. It focuses on what is objectively different
about the offering.

Why It Matters
A brand can be distinctive without being different, but being different

Salience is the measure of how readily a brand comes to mind in buying
situations. Building salience requires consistent, memorable, and widely
distributed communication. The principle is simple: say less, more often, across
more channels. This means focusing on a few meaningful brand attributes and
repeating them until they become second nature to the consumer. Consistency
across touchpoints is key, as fragmented messaging dilutes the brand’s ability to
create strong memory structures.
To achieve salience, brands need to leverage Distinctive Brand Assets (DBA)—
the logos, colors, typography, taglines, and even sounds that make a brand
unmistakably itself. These assets should not only be memorable but also
intrinsically tied to the brand’s identity. For example, KFC’s red-and-white
buckets, Colonel Sanders iconography, and “finger-lickin’ good” tagline have
helped it dominate consumer recognition.
Salience is the result of being consistently distinctive. Brands that master both
differentiation and distinctiveness ensure that they are not just seen but also
remembered and chosen.

and mission (e.g. sustainability, inclusivity, or social impact).

Communicate One Clear Message
Simplicity wins in branding. Once you’ve identified what makes you unique, distil

It’s not enough to have a distinctive idea—you need to reinforce it at every

Hope

The Power of Emotional Triggers
and Brain Chemistry
Emotional triggers in marketing—whether a sense of hope, belonging, or
relief— are not just abstract concepts; they activate specific neurochemical
reactions in the brain that influence behavior. These biochemical processes help
explain why some messages resonate so powerfully, driving us to act or buy
without fully understanding why.
z Dopamine: The Driver of Desire and Reward
 What It Does: Dopamine is the brain’s “reward” neurotransmitter, released
when we anticipate or achieve something pleasurable. It fuels motivation,
makes us feel good, and reinforces behaviors we associate with positive
outcomes.
 In Shopping: Advertisements or products that evoke hope—such as
the promise of a better life, a solution to a problem, or the excitement of
exclusivity—trigger dopamine. This creates a sense of anticipation,
motivating shoppers to click “buy now” or pursue the product to experience
that reward.
74

z Oxytocin: Building Trust and Connection
 What It Does: Oxytocin, often called the “bonding hormone,” fosters
feelings of trust, safety, and connection. It’s the same chemical released during
social bonding moments like hugging or sharing meaningful experiences.
 In Shopping: Brands that emphasize belonging, community, or shared
values tap into oxytocin. For example, messaging that shows how a
product connects you to a group or aligns with your identity fosters trust and
strengthens emotional attachment to the brand.

z Cortisol: Reducing Stress for Comfort and Empowerment
 What It Does: Cortisol is the body’s stress hormone, which spikes during
moments of tension or anxiety. Reducing cortisol levels creates a sense of relief
and calm, allowing people to feel more in control.
 In Shopping: Products or services that offer relief—whether from physical
discomfort, emotional stress, or practical challenges—help lower cortisol. For
example, messaging like “easy returns” or “hassle- free solutions” reassures
customers, making them feel empowered and confident in their decision.

z Serotonin: Reinforcing Positivity and Contentment
 What It Does: Serotonin stabilizes mood, promoting feelings of happiness
and well-being. It’s often linked to moments of gratitude, satisfaction, or
accomplishment.
 In Shopping: Marketing that taps into gratitude—such as testimonials,
success stories, or rewards programs—can trigger serotonin, leaving
customers with a sense of contentment about their purchase decision.

75

Biochemistry’s Impact on Decision-Making
These neurochemical responses often bypass our logical, deliberate “System 2”
thinking (as described by Daniel Kahneman) and activate our automatic, emotional
“System 1” processes. This is why emotional appeals in marketing can have such an
immediate impact, driving decisions before we even consciously process them. For
example:
 An ad featuring a happy family sharing a meal (belonging) creates an
emotional connection, activating oxytocin and making the product feel
trustworthy.
 A limited-time offer (anticipation) sparks dopamine, motivating immediate
action to capture the reward.
 A guarantee of satisfaction (relief) reduces cortisol, making the choice feel
risk-free.

The Subconscious Search for Emotional Comfort
At the core of these processes is the subconscious drive to feel good, safe, and
connected. Shoppers aren’t just looking for products; they’re seeking emotional
highs, comfort, and reassurance that align with their deeper desires. By
understanding how biochemistry influences behavior, brands can craft messaging,
experiences, and products that not only meet customer needs but also resonate on
a chemical and emotional level, fostering loyalty and driving long-term success.

THE CUSTOMER JOURNEY

In marketing, guiding customers from their first awareness of a product to
making a purchase is a nuanced process. Rather than following a straight path,
the customer journey unfolds in a series of interconnected experiences, shaped
by both conscious and subconscious needs. Successful brands recognize these
emotional triggers and respond in ways that make the journey feel seamless
and personal.
In this chapter, we’ll explore how to map this experience using emotional
touchpoints—key moments that influence decision-making. We’ll also discuss
how a brand’s voice, including tone, language, design, and imagery, shapes
customer perception at every stage.
Customers rarely move in a linear progression. Instead, they encounter pivotal
moments—what we call “signposts”—that guide them toward a solution
that meets their needs or desires. The challenge for brands isn’t just to create
outstanding products but to ensure they are positioned as the most relevant
choice at these defining moments.

Identifying Pain Points in the
Customer Journey
Customers often experience frustration, confusion, or discomfort as they search
for a resolution. These issues create opportunities for brands to introduce
solutions that address not just the functional task but also the emotional
struggle behind it. It’s by recognizing these problems that brands can truly
create value for customers.

Don’t Look for New Products—
Look for Problems
Rather than focusing on creating new products for the sake of innovation,
brands should focus on identifying desires or problems in their customers’ lives.
137

By doing so, they can develop products that are directly tied to solving real pain
points, increasing the product’s perceived value in the customer’s mind.
For example, Dyson didn’t set out to create just another vacuum cleaner. Dyson
identified the frustration people felt with traditional vacuum designs—losing
suction, being difficult to maneuver, and constantly clogging. The result was a
vacuum that eliminated these pain points, positioning Dyson as an innovator in
the market.

Creating Value in the Customer’s Mind
Obviously, customers value products that solve their struggles and make
their lives easier. By focusing on these pain points, brands can position their
products as solutions that deliver real emotional value. This emotional value is
often more important to the customer than the product’s features or technical
specifications.
Take, for example, Apple’s AirPods. While technically advanced, their true
value comes from the convenience and freedom they provide—no tangled
wires, seamless Bluetooth connection, and easy portability. Apple understood
the underlying frustration with traditional wired headphones and designed a
product that addressed this pain point, creating a sense of ease and mobility
that resonates emotionally with customers.

The Customer Journey: A Path
Toward Emotional Fulfillment
The customer journey typically consists of several stages, often called the sales
funnel or purchase funnel. While every customer’s journey is unique, the common
stages include:
 Awareness: The customer realizes they have a problem or need.
 Consideration: The customer starts exploring possible solutions.
138

 Decision: The customer chooses which solution or product to purchase.
 Retention: After the purchase, the brand works to keep the customer’s loyalty.
 Advocacy: A loyal customer becomes an advocate for the brand,
recommending it to others.

Customers NEVER buy randomly. Each decision to buy is accompanied by
motivations and emotional triggers that help move the customer forward.
Understanding these motivations is crucial for brands looking to create
authentic and effective customer experiences. These motivations serve as the
“Signposts” that guide the way.
People use mental shortcuts, known as heuristics, to simplify decision-making.
These shortcuts help customers make quick decisions without fully processing all
the available information. However, heuristics are often influenced by cognitive
biases, such as:
 Social proof: People are more likely to follow the behavior of others,
especially in uncertain situations.
 Authority bias: People tend to trust and follow the recommendations of
experts or authority figures.

139

 Loss aversion: People are more motivated to avoid losses than to achieve
equivalent gains, making scarcity and urgency powerful motivators.
When faced with too many options, they rely on heuristics to make quick
decisions. This is why brands that focus on one clear message or benefit tend
to be more memorable. Being distinctive does not necessarily mean being
complex—it means being easily identifiable and emotionally resonant.
The way information is presented, or framed, significantly impacts how people
perceive it. For instance, framing a product as “95% effective” is more persuasive
than framing it as “5% ineffective”, even though both statements convey
the same information. Similarly, context influences decisions—customers’
preferences can shift based on environmental cues, timing, or the emotions they
are experiencing at the time.
Humans are inherently social beings, and much of our decision-making is
influenced by our desire to belong to specific groups. We are more likely to
align with brands that reflect our values, aspirations, and social identities. This
is why many brands focus on creating communities or aligning with cultural
movements.

Signposts on the Customer Journey
In the IDEA Framework, I call these emotional triggers and mental shortcuts
“Signposts” because they act as key emotional moments that guide customers
from one stage of the journey to the next.
 Awareness: At this stage, the customer realizes they have a problem or need.
The emotional Signpost could be curiosity or frustration, as they seek a solution
to something bothering them.
Example: A customer realizes they need a new skincare routine because they
feel self-conscious about their skin texture. The emotional Signpost is the desire
for self-confidence and self-care. A brand that recognizes this need, rather
than just selling a generic “skincare solution,” connects on a deeper level.
140

 Consideration: In this stage, the customer actively looks for solutions,
comparing options. The emotional Signpost might be hope or anticipation as
they seek a product that will meet their needs.
Example: A customer considering different fitness apps wants to feel
healthier. One app, using imagery of real people and a warm tone of voice, taps
into the customer’s aspiration, creating an emotional connection.
 Decision: By now, the customer has narrowed their options and is emotionally
ready to make a choice. The Signpost is often trust or relief, as they want to
feel confident in their decision. They should be anticipating their “Shopper
High” as they look forward to making their purchase.
Example: A customer deciding between two travel companies chooses the
one that emphasizes customer testimonials and offers a clear refund policy. The
emotional Signpost of trust reassures them of a positive experience.
 Retention: After purchasing, the customer seeks satisfaction and validation.
They want to feel that they made the right choice.
Example: A clothing brand sends a personalized thank-you email after
a purchase, including styling tips for the items. This gesture reinforces the
customer’s satisfaction and makes them feel valued.
 Advocacy: In this final stage, the customer feels so connected to the brand
that they want to share their positive experience with others. The emotional
Signpost might be pride or belonging.
Example: Customers of a tech company post online reviews or share unboxing
videos because they feel proud to be part of the brand’s community, not
because they were asked.
Each Signpost reflects an emotional need that customers want to fulfill. Brands
that recognize and address these Signposts through their messaging and customer
experience can guide customers smoothly through the journey.

141

The Role of Your Brand Voice
in the Customer Journey
A critical component of guiding customers through these emotional Signposts
is the brand’s voice. The brand voice encompasses the overall personality that
the brand conveys, including tone of voice, language style, and design elements
such as color, typography, and imagery. A well-crafted brand voice can tap into
the emotions and motivations driving the customer’s journey.

Tone of Voice: Reflecting Empathy and Understanding
Tone of voice refers to how a brand communicates, not just what it says.
Different stages of the customer journey require different tones. A friendly,
reassuring tone might work during the decision stage, while a confident tone
could be more effective in the consideration stage.
 Example: A financial services brand might use a calming and educational
tone at the awareness stage to ease fears, then shift to a more empowering
tone during the decision phase to boost confidence.

Language Style: Simple, Emotional, and Relatable
The style of language plays a key role in creating emotional connections. Brands
that use simple, relatable, and emotional language make it easier for customers
to understand and feel connected to their product.
 Example: Airbnb uses warm, inviting language such as “Live like a local” to
tap into the emotional need for belonging. This simplicity makes customers
feel like they’re part of a community.

142

Conclusion: Crafting a Seamless Emotional Journey
The customer journey is an emotional process, shaped by motivations and
needs that guide each decision. By recognizing these emotional triggers—or,
Signposts—brands can create a journey that feels authentic and personal. A
strong brand voice, reflecting empathy, simplicity, and thoughtful design, is key
to guiding customers through this journey. Brands that align their voice with
these emotional Signposts will build lasting relationships with their customers,
transforming them from first-time buyers into loyal advocates.

Overcoming Barriers: Addressing
Anxieties and Frictions in the Journey
One of the biggest obstacles to product adoption is customer anxiety—the fear
of trying something new or changing from an existing solution. To encourage
customers to choose your product, you must address these anxieties head-on
and reduce friction points throughout the customer journey.
People are often reluctant to change because they are comfortable with their
existing habits. This is described as the “habit of the present” by Bob Moesta in
his Jobs-To-Be-Done Framework. Even if a new product offers better features,
customers may feel anxious about learning how to use it or adapting to a new
process.
Brands can address these anxieties by framing their product in a way that feels
familiar and accessible, while also demonstrating how the new solution will lead
to a better outcome. Additionally, brands can reduce friction by making the
transition as easy as possible—whether through intuitive design, step-by-step
onboarding, or clear instructional content.
 Example: When Apple launched the iPhone, there was anxiety about
switching from traditional phones to a touchscreen device. Apple alleviated
143

this anxiety by making the iPhone’s interface highly intuitive, using design
elements that made the transition feel natural. The introduction of the App

enhanced their daily lives.
 Example: When Slack entered the workplace communication market, many
companies were hesitant to move away from email, fearing disruption to
existing workflows. Slack reduced this anxiety by designing an interface
that felt familiar, integrating seamlessly with tools like email and project
management software, and offering an intuitive onboarding experience. By
emphasizing improved collaboration without forcing users to abandon their
current habits, Slack eased the transition and became a widely adopted
workplace tool.
