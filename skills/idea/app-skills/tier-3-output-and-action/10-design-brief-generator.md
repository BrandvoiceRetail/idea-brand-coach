# SKILL — The Design Brief Generator

## Purpose
Translates a Decision Trigger into an actionable design brief — image brief, copy brief, A+/landing-page brief — **ready to hand to a designer or VA without any additional interpretation.** The moment the diagnostic becomes commercial. A prescription, not a menu: this is the image, this is the copy, this is why, this is where it goes.

> The brief must be specific enough that a designer who has never spoken to the brand owner understands exactly what is needed, why, and what it must achieve — without understanding the IDEA Framework, the Trust Gap, or the Decision Trigger architecture.

---

## 1. Brief structure (all four components required, generated in sequence)

**Component A — Brief context (for the recipient).** One paragraph to the designer/VA: what was found and what the brief fixes. No framework terminology. Example: *"Your customer has tried multiple products in this category and been disappointed. They arrive at your listing carrying past failure. The brief below is designed to address that before anything else is said about the product."*

**Component B — Image brief.** Specific instructions for hero image and image stack: what the hero must communicate in the first three seconds of scanning (before any text), which buyer-state signal it must activate, the **emotional register** (not the compositional style — that's the designer's job), and the specific trust signal it must contain. Does not specify colour/font/composition unless directly linked to the trigger. *Specifies what must be communicated, not how it must look.*

**Component C — Copy brief.** Headline instruction (one sentence — what it must do), bullet 1/2/3 instructions (what trust signal each leads with and why), CTA instruction (what it must say + the emotional register it matches). Verbatim copy suggestions where the trigger makes them obvious.

**Component D — Placement instruction.** Where in the listing/landing page the primary trust-signal change must happen first (source: Skill 09 Component 3, translated to plain language). Example: *"The change that will have the most immediate impact on conversion is the hero image headline and bullet 1. Everything else can follow in a second iteration once this is tested."*

## 2. How the trigger determines the brief

| Decision Trigger | Image brief direction | Copy brief direction |
|---|---|---|
| **Permission** | Hero communicates expertise/authority immediately — credentials, certification, clinical imagery, or expert endorsement visible without reading text. | Headline leads with the authority claim. Bullet 1: the specific proof point. CTA: "See the evidence". No emotional language in the first two bullets. |
| **Recognition** | Hero mirrors the customer's emotional reality before the product appears — the struggle, the moment before, or the relief; not the product benefit. | Headline mirrors the experience exactly ("If you have tried everything else and nothing worked…"). Bullet 1: acknowledge the failure state. Bullet 2: why this is different. Bullet 3: proof. |
| **Identity** | Hero signals cultural belonging and aspiration — the product in the context of who the customer wants to be; community/lifestyle/status imagery. | Headline: identity claim ("For people who…"). Bullet 1: identity signal. Bullet 2: product as identity confirmation. Bullet 3: community/social signal. |
| **Belonging** | Hero shows community and brand purpose — people, mission, story; not just product. | Headline: mission/values statement. Bullet 1: brand/founder story. Bullet 2: community/shared values. Bullet 3: product as expression of those values. |
| **Momentum** | Hero leads with social-proof volume — number of customers, reviews, repeat-purchase rate; make the scale visible immediately. | Headline: social-proof number/claim ("Trusted by X customers"). Bullet 1: review count/rating. Bullet 2: specific outcome from reviews. Bullet 3: final risk removal. |
| **Fear-of-Loss** | Hero communicates the cost of inaction visually — before/after, time-sensitive outcome, the consequence of delay. | Headline frames the cost of waiting, not the benefit of buying ("Every day without…"). Bullet 1: outcome timeline. Bullet 2: what customers who delayed say. Bullet 3: urgency without false scarcity. |

## 3. What the brief must never contain
- Framework terminology: IDEA, Trust Gap, Decision Trigger, buyer state, CAPTURE.
- Buyer-state names: Assessor, Protector, Expresser, Connector.
- Academic references: Kahneman, Zaltman, neuroanatomy, System 1/2.
- Options for the designer to choose between — the brief gives one direction.
- Vague adjectives without commercial purpose ("warm", "friendly", "professional") unless what they must communicate is specified.
- Instructions the designer cannot execute without another conversation.

## 4. Worked example — Guyology Labs (Recognition trigger)
*Trust Gap 62/100. Primary gap: Empathetic (8/25). Dominant buyer state: The Protector (internal). Primary Decision Trigger: Recognition.*

**A — Context:** Your customer has almost certainly tried multiple hair-growth products before finding yours. They arrive carrying past disappointment and the fear that this will be another dead end. The brief acknowledges that experience before any product claims. The current listing is strong on clinical evidence but leads with the answer before acknowledging the question. That is what this brief fixes.

**B — Image:** The hero must communicate that the brand understands the emotional journey of hair loss — frustration, failed attempts, fragile hope — before what the product does. Consider a split image showing the emotional before-state (through expression and context, not literally) alongside the product. The dermatologist element must be visible at a glance — the authority signal that overrides scepticism. Certified badges in the hero, not secondary images.

**C — Copy:** Headline mirrors the experience: "For people who have tried everything else." Bullet 1 acknowledges the failure state, not the benefit: "You're not imagining it. Most hair-growth products don't deliver clinical-grade AnaGain™ concentration." Bullet 2: the clinical differentiator. Bullet 3: the dermatologist endorsement with specificity. CTA: "See why dermatologists recommend this".

**D — Placement:** Most immediate impact is the hero headline and bullet 1. The clinical data/imagery stack is already strong and should not change this iteration. The AnaGain concentration data belongs in bullet 3, not bullet 1.
