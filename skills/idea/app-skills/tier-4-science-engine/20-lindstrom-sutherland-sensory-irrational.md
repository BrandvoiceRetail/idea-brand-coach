## 1. Lindstrom — emotion beats logic, sensed before it is read

As the book states it (pp. 69–70):

> "emotional triggers are stimuli that resonate with a customer's feelings and subconscious desires. As Martin Lindstrom explains in *Buyology: Truth and Lies About Why We Buy*, emotion outweighs logic in most purchasing decisions."

The book positions this Lindstrom citation directly alongside Zaltman's 95% statistic. Together they anchor the argument that strong brands **activate emotional triggers** rather than rely on rational appeals — and the framework's wider teaching that brands sell *feelings, not features*: people don't buy products for their functionality, they buy them for how they make them feel, and that feeling is assembled from associations (colour, imagery, status, safety) that land before any text is read.

**The engine consequence:** the image stack does most of its work below language. A shopper scanning a listing forms a feeling about the brand in the first seconds — from the hero image, the colour, the composition — long before they read a bullet. The hero brief must therefore be written to communicate the *feeling and the trust signal* pre-language, not to caption the product.

## 2. Sutherland — irrational value, perception over reality *(reference-only)*

The architecture pairs Lindstrom with **Sutherland** for the complementary half: **irrational value** — that perceived value is not the same as objective value, and the logical case for a product almost never sells it on its own. Two listings with identical specs convert differently because of how they are *perceived*, not because of what they objectively are.

This half is grounded in the architecture summary and Sutherland's place in the book's bibliography (*Alchemy: The Dark Art and Curious Science of Creating Magic in Brands, Business, and Life*), which the book lists but does not teach in-text. Treat it as the **internal licence to lead the Distinctive pillar with perception rather than the spec sheet** — consistent with the book's own teaching that small brands rarely win on functional *difference* and should instead claim a memorable, distinctive position (Ries & Trout). Do not attribute specific *Alchemy* claims to the book; it has none. Use the principle, not the proof.

## 3. The two operational consequences

| Consequence | What it means for the engine | Where it lands |
|---|---|---|
| **Imagery and colour work pre-language (Lindstrom).** | The feeling about a brand is built from sensory/associative cues that register before text. The hero is the first — and often only — trust signal a scanning customer processes. | Drives the **image brief** in the Design Brief Generator (Skill 10): specify the *emotional register* and the *trust signal the hero must communicate in the first three seconds*, not the composition. The look is the designer's job; what it must *make the customer feel* is the brief's. |
| **Perceived value ≠ objective value (Sutherland).** | The logical case rarely sells alone; perception is the lever. A more distinctive, more confidently *felt* presentation outperforms a more accurate but generic one. | Justifies — internally — leading the **Distinctive** pillar (and the Identity trigger) with perception and memorability over a feature/spec recitation. Reinforces the IDEA core point that "distinctive" means *memorably, relevantly different*, not merely different. |

## Coaching Application
Use to push the **image brief** toward sensory/associative cues that work before language (Lindstrom) and to justify — internally — leading the **Distinctive** pillar with perception over the logical case (Sutherland).

- When briefing a hero or image stack, write for the *first three seconds of scanning*: the feeling and the single trust signal it must land pre-text. Specify what it must *communicate*, never the colour/font/composition (that is the designer's craft) — exactly as the Design Brief Generator requires.
- When a brand owner wants to win by listing more or better specs, lean the other way: a distinctive, confidently-felt presentation beats an accurate but generic one. Coach toward owning one focused feeling (Volvo owns safety) rather than out-speccing rivals.
- On the Identity trigger / Distinctive pillar, let *perception* lead — who the customer becomes by choosing this brand — not the functional case.
- **Never** surface the sources by name, the books, or the "sensory branding" / "irrational value" framing. Output the precision, not the proof.
