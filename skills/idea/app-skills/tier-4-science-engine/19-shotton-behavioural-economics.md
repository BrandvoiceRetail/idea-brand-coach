## 1. What this skill is for
The IDEA framework's central claim is that **trust is the deciding factor** and that the buying decision is overwhelmingly emotional and subconscious. Shotton's contribution to the engine is the *commercial validation layer*: a set of small, evidence-backed behavioural effects that make the difference between a trust signal that lands and one that bounces — at the level of a number, a word, an admission, an option set.

It is the engine, not the pitch. These effects tune the **Decision Trigger** (Skill 09) and the words the **CAPTURE Framework** (Skill 11) puts on the page so that the trust the framework is trying to build is actually *received*. The framework decides *which* lever to pull; this skill sharpens *how* the proof for that lever is expressed.

## 2. The four effects

| Effect | What it says (drafted from the master-table description + general behavioural-economics framing) | The precision it adds |
|---|---|---|
| **Precise vs round numbers** | Specific, non-round figures read as more credible than round ones, because precision signals that a real measurement (not a marketing estimate) stands behind the claim. | Trust-signal copy and proof claims should carry the *exact* figure, not the tidied-up round one. |
| **The pratfall effect** | A small, honest admission of imperfection *increases* trust rather than reducing it — a flawless pitch reads as a sales pitch; one genuine limitation makes the rest believable. | One deliberate, true imperfection makes every other claim more credible — applied to authenticity copy, not as a gimmick. |
| **Social norms** | People take the behaviour of similar others as evidence of the right choice; framing proof as *what people like them already do* is more persuasive than an abstract benefit. | Review strategy and social proof should frame volume and similarity ("people in your situation chose this"), not just a star rating. |
| **The compromise effect** | Faced with three options, people gravitate to the middle one; the presence of a higher and a lower flank makes the target choice feel like the safe, sensible pick. | Option-set and pricing-tier presentation can be structured so the intended choice sits in the reassuring middle. |

> These four are the *only* effects in scope for this skill until Trevor's detailed doc expands it. Treat anything beyond them as out of scope.

## Coaching Application
Use this skill to sharpen concrete tactics *inside* the brief and the copy, never as a topic:
- Put the **precise** figure in a proof claim, not the rounded one ("reduces noise by 31%", not "by about a third").
- Add **one honest, true imperfection** to authenticity copy to make the rest believable — never invent a flaw, never use it as a gimmick.
- Frame social proof as **what similar buyers already did**, not an abstract benefit — feed it from the review corpus (Skill 07).
- Where a tiered/option presentation exists, structure it so the intended choice sits in the **reassuring middle**.

Apply only the effect that reinforces the dominant Decision Trigger for this brand; do not stack effects (the book's "pick the dominant trigger, don't stack" rule applies here too). Never name the effect, the discipline, or the source in anything the user sees — the user receives a sharper claim, not a behavioural-economics lesson.
