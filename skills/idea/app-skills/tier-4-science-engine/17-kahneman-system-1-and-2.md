# SKILL — Kahneman: System 1 and System 2

> Draft from Trevor's ideas + the book — pending his authoritative detailed doc + book expansion.

## Purpose
**System 1** (fast, automatic, emotional, subconscious) and **System 2** (slow, deliberate, rational, conscious). Why most brand communication is written for System 2 and ignored by System 1; how the IDEA pillars map to each; and why trust signals must work in **three seconds of System 1 scanning** before System 2 engages.

## 1. The two systems, as the book uses them
The book draws on Daniel Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* to explain *why* emotional appeals work so fast: they bypass deliberate reasoning and act on the automatic mind first.

| System | Character | In the buying moment |
|---|---|---|
| **System 1** | Fast, automatic, emotional, subconscious. Always running; cannot be switched off. | Forms the first impression of a listing — the hero image, the headline, the price presentation — and reacts with a feeling *before* any conscious reading begins. |
| **System 2** | Slow, deliberate, rational, conscious. Effortful; engages only when prompted. | Reads the bullets, weighs the specs, compares alternatives — but only *after* System 1 has already leant toward or away from the brand. |

The book's two anchoring quotations:

> "Most decisions, as highlighted by Daniel Kahneman in his book *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, occur in the subconscious, driven by emotional and psychological needs rather than pure logic." *(p. 69)*

> "These neurochemical responses often bypass our logical, deliberate 'System 2' thinking (as described by Daniel Kahneman) and activate our automatic, emotional 'System 1' processes. This is why emotional appeals in marketing can have such an immediate impact, driving decisions before we even consciously process them." *(p. 75)*

This is the engine under the 95% claim (Skill 16, Zaltman): System 1 is *where* the subconscious decision happens. The book does not treat the two systems as separable strategies — it treats System 1 as the gatekeeper. If System 1 is not won in the first moment, System 2 is never properly engaged.

## 2. Why most brand communication is written for the wrong system
The recurring commercial failure the book describes: a brand pours its effort into feature-dense, specification-led, rational copy — communication aimed squarely at System 2 — and is ignored, because the customer's System 1 decided in the first seconds that the brand did not feel right. A listing that is all specs and price is optimising for the deliberate mind that arrives last, and starving the automatic mind that decides first. This is the same failure the **Empathetic** pillar names: *communicating rationally in an emotionally driven decision* — speaking only to the rational few is talking to the wrong brain.

## 3. The biochemical layer the book attaches to System 1
The book grounds System 1 in brain chemistry: emotional triggers fire specific neurochemicals, and those responses are exactly what bypass System 2. The engine uses these pairings to choose *which feeling* a message should aim for — never to explain them to the user.

| Trigger (the cue) | Chemistry it fires | What it does in the moment |
|---|---|---|
| **Belonging** — happy-family / community cue | **Oxytocin** | Builds trust and connection; the product feels safe and "for people like me". |
| **Anticipation** — limited-time / exclusivity cue | **Dopamine** | Sparks desire and motivates immediate action to capture the reward. |
| **Relief** — guarantee / hassle-free cue | Lowers **cortisol** | Reduces stress; makes the choice feel risk-free and in the customer's control. |
| **Gratitude** — testimonial / social-proof cue | **Serotonin** | Reinforces positivity and contentment about the decision already made. |

Beneath all four sits the book's single-sentence read on what the customer is actually shopping for: *the subconscious drive to feel good, safe, and connected.* The job of a System-1-ready message is to create **anticipation of that future feeling** — itself a biochemical event that moves the decision before logic catches up.

## 4. How the IDEA pillars map to each system
| Pillar | System it must win | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **I — Insight** | System 1 | The emotional "why" the customer cannot always articulate operates below language; surface-level, functional copy reaches only the deliberate mind. |
| **E — Empathetic** | System 1 | "They get me" is an automatic, emotional response, not a logical analysis. Empathy is felt before it is reasoned. |
| **D — Distinctive** | System 1 | Memorable, relevant difference is recognised instantly by the automatic mind; the customer doesn't need to compare to feel it's theirs. |
| **A — Authentic** | System 1, then System 2 | First-impression credibility is a System-1 judgement made in seconds; the reviews and detail that System 2 later reads must *confirm* that instinct, not have to create it. |

The through-line: **all four pillars are won or lost in System 1 first.** Tier 4 exists to keep every Tier 2 and Tier 3 output honest to that ordering — emotion-first, instantly legible, the rational support arriving second.

## 5. The three-second standard
Because System 1 decides on first impression and cannot be switched off, the engine holds a hard internal standard: the **hero must do its emotional work in roughly three seconds of System-1 scanning, before System 2 engages.** Whatever the customer feels in those seconds is the decision the rest of the listing only confirms or fails to rescue. This standard weights *which* trust signals must be instantly legible (not buried in the bullets) and is the internal justification for the "three-second hero" requirement in the Design Brief Generator (Skill 10). A high-impression / low-click listing is a System-1 failure: the first-impression stimulus is not triggering the right feeling fast enough.

## Coaching Application
Use to keep every output honest to the ordering **emotion first, logic second**, and to enforce instant legibility of the signals that matter.

- When a brand owner over-invests in feature-heavy, specification-led copy at the expense of feeling, the engine should lean **emotion-first**: the hero and headline must trigger the right feeling immediately, with the facts supporting underneath — never the reverse. (Surface this as commercial advice — "lead with the feeling, support with the facts" — never as System 1/2.)
- When diagnosing a high-impressions / low-clicks listing, read it as a first-impression failure: the hero, headline, or price presentation is not firing the right emotional response before the customer reads anything.
- When choosing *which* feeling a message should aim for, use the trigger → chemistry pairings (belonging→trust, anticipation→action, relief→risk-free, gratitude→contentment) to shape the Decision Trigger™ and Design Brief — surfaced as the message, never as the mechanism.
- Enforce the **three-second hero** standard in the image brief (Skill 10) and weight which trust signals must be instantly legible for the **Authentic** pillar.
- **Never** surface the model by name, the system labels, the source, or the neurochemistry. Output the precision, not the proof.
