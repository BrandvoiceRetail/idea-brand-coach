# SKILL — Zaltman: The Subconscious Purchase Decision

> Draft from Trevor's ideas + the book — pending his authoritative detailed doc + book expansion.

## Purpose
The **95% principle** — why the majority of purchase decisions occur below conscious awareness; why review language is post-rationalisation, not cause; why demographic avatars fail. The commercial implication: a brand that only speaks to what customers consciously *say* they want is speaking to 5% of the decision.

## 1. The principle, as the book states it
Professor Gerald Zaltman of Harvard University, in *How Customers Think: Essential Insights into the Mind of the Market* (2003), holds that **up to 95% of purchasing decisions are driven by emotions, not rational thought.** Those decisions occur in the subconscious mind; we typically justify them afterwards by constructing logical explanations.

The book builds on Freud — human behaviour is shaped by the subconscious, by hidden biases and psychological triggers we are not aware of — and uses Zaltman to establish the master justification for the entire emotion-first framework: **emotions and subconscious drivers dominate how people perceive, evaluate, and ultimately choose.** This is the scientific anchor cited whenever someone insists buying is rational.

## 2. The three operational consequences

| Consequence | What it means for the engine | Where it lands |
|---|---|---|
| **The decision happens first, the reasons come second.** | Emotional response leads; logic arrives afterwards to justify a choice already made. Two buyers with identical budgets and needs choose differently because their *feeling* about the brand differs. | Weights the **Insight** and **Empathetic** pillars: a listing that is all specs and price is optimising for the ~5% that happens last. |
| **Review language is post-rationalisation, not cause.** | What a customer *writes* is the logical story they built after the subconscious decision — useful as evidence, but it is the justification, not the driver. The "emotional why" sits beneath the stated reason. | Tells Review Corpus Analysis (Skill 07) and Purchase Motivation (Skill 08) to read *beneath* the stated reason for the emotional driver, not to take review text at face value. |
| **Demographic avatars fail.** | Age, income and location do not predict the emotional why. Two people in the same demographic make opposite choices for emotional reasons. The decision is subconscious and emotional, not categorical. | Justifies — internally — why Avatar 2.0™ is a forensic-behavioural portrait, not a demographic segment (demographics live in the Brand Context profile, not the Avatar). |

## 3. The named technique the book attaches to it
The book names **Zaltman's metaphor elicitation technique** as the method for surfacing the "emotional why" that drives purchase behaviour, and ties it directly to Avatar 2.0™. Internally, this is the licence for the Avatar pipeline to dig past the literal complaint or compliment to the metaphor underneath it (e.g. "flimsy" → a fear of being let down; "looks the part" → a wish to be seen a certain way). Surface the sharper Avatar, never the technique.

## Coaching Application
Use to keep every diagnosis anchored in the emotional/subconscious driver (the **Insight** and **Empathetic** pillars) and to justify — internally — why the Avatar is forensic-behavioural, not demographic.

- When a brand owner insists their customers "decide rationally" or builds a listing that is all specs and price, the engine should lean **emotion-first**: lead with the feeling and the outcome, back it with logic — never the reverse. (Surface this as commercial advice, not as the 95% figure.)
- When reading reviews, treat the stated reason as the *justification* and look for the emotional driver beneath it; let that driver shape the Decision Trigger™ rather than the surface wording.
- When tempted to segment by demographics, don't — the subconscious, emotional nature of the decision is exactly why a forensic-behavioural Avatar outperforms a demographic one.
- **Never** surface the principle by name, the figure, the source, or the technique. Output the precision, not the proof.
