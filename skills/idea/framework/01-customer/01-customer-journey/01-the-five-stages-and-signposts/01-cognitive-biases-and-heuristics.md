# SKILL — Heuristics and Cognitive Biases in the Journey

## Purpose
The behavioral-science engine under the funnel: customers use mental shortcuts (heuristics) to decide quickly, and those shortcuts are shaped by predictable cognitive biases. Knowing them lets a brand frame its message so the shortcut works *for* the purchase.

---

## Heuristics and the Key Biases
> "People use mental shortcuts, known as heuristics, to simplify decision-making. These shortcuts help customers make quick decisions without fully processing all the available information. However, heuristics are often influenced by cognitive biases, such as:"
>
> - **Social proof:** People are more likely to follow the behavior of others, especially in uncertain situations.
> - **Authority bias:** People tend to trust and follow the recommendations of experts or authority figures.
> - **Loss aversion:** People are more motivated to avoid losses than to achieve equivalent gains, making scarcity and urgency powerful motivators.

## One Clear Message
> "When faced with too many options, they rely on heuristics to make quick decisions. This is why brands that focus on one clear message or benefit tend to be more memorable. Being distinctive does not necessarily mean being complex—it means being easily identifiable and emotionally resonant."

## The Framing Effect
> "The way information is presented, or framed, significantly impacts how people perceive it. For instance, framing a product as '95% effective' is more persuasive than framing it as '5% ineffective', even though both statements convey the same information. Similarly, context influences decisions—customers' preferences can shift based on environmental cues, timing, or the emotions they are experiencing at the time."

## Belonging and Social Identity
> "Humans are inherently social beings, and much of our decision-making is influenced by our desire to belong to specific groups. We are more likely to align with brands that reflect our values, aspirations, and social identities. This is why many brands focus on creating communities or aligning with cultural movements."

## Coaching Application
Audit a listing or campaign against these levers: Is there social proof (reviews, counts)? An authority cue (expert, certification)? A loss-aversion trigger (scarcity, deadline)? Is the message framed as a gain (95% effective) rather than a loss? Is there one clear benefit, not a complex pile? And does it signal a group the customer wants to belong to?
