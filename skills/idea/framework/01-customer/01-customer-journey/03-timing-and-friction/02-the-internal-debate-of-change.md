# SKILL — The Internal Debate of Change

## Purpose
Customers run a private push-and-pull debate before switching: the pull toward a better solution vs. the fear of the unknown and the comfort of the familiar. Even a clearly better product loses if the transition feels risky. Brands win by smoothing the transition and addressing the fear directly.

---

## The Push and Pull
> "As mentioned earlier, change causes anxiety, and customers often undergo an internal debate about whether to switch to a new solution. This debate can happen over a long period, and brands need to recognize that the customer's resistance to change is often rooted in the fear of the unknown or the risk of losing what they currently have."

> "Even if a new product offers a better solution, customers may worry about the transition or the learning curve associated with adopting something new. Familiarity with the current product, even if it's suboptimal, creates a sense of comfort. Brands need to help customers overcome this anxiety by making the transition as smooth as possible and addressing these concerns directly."

## Example — Microsoft Windows 7 → 10
> "Microsoft had to manage significant customer anxiety when transitioning from Windows 7 to Windows 10. Many users were hesitant to upgrade, fearing changes in the user interface and compatibility issues. To ease this transition, Microsoft offered free upgrades for a period, provided extensive support, and promoted the benefits of improved security and functionality to overcome user resistance."

## Coaching Application
Name both sides of the customer's internal debate: the pull (the better outcome you offer) and the pull-back (familiarity, learning curve, fear of loss). Then lower the cost of switching — free upgrade window, hand-holding support, explicit benefit messaging — and address the specific fears out loud. A suboptimal-but-familiar incumbent is a stronger competitor than its features suggest.
