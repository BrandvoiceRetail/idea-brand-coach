# SKILL — Customizing the Sales Process by Customer Mode

## Purpose
Friction arises when the sales process doesn't match the customer's current stage. Apply design thinking to give the right amount of information and engagement for where the customer is — defined by three modes: Passive looking, Active looking, and Deciding. Each mode needs a different move.

---

## Match Engagement to Stage
> "Friction points can also arise when the sales process doesn't match the customer's current stage in their journey. Customers who are still exploring options may be turned off by aggressive sales tactics, while those ready to make a purchase might get frustrated if the buying process is too slow or complicated. Design thinking can be applied to the sales process by offering customers the right amount of information and engagement based on where they are in their journey."

## The Three Customer Modes (verbatim)
> - **Passive looking customers** are just learning and browsing. They need educational content and non-pressured engagement to help them understand their problem better.
> - **Active looking customers** are solution-aware and need clear comparisons and specific information about how your product solves their problem.
> - **Deciding customers** are ready to buy and need reassurances, such as testimonials, guarantees, or easy paths to purchase.

> "By understanding where customers are in their process, brands can offer the appropriate level of engagement and move them toward the purchase with minimal friction."

## Coaching Application
Tag each touchpoint by the mode it serves. Don't push a passive browser with hard-sell tactics, and don't slow a deciding buyer with education they no longer need. Passive → educational, non-pressured content. Active → comparisons and specifics. Deciding → testimonials, guarantees, frictionless checkout. Mismatched engagement is itself a friction point.
