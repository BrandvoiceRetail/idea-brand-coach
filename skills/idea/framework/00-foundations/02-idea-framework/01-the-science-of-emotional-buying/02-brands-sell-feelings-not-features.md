# SKILL — Brands Sell Feelings, Not Features

## Purpose
The core branding move: people don't buy products for their functionality — they buy them for how they make them feel. Carries the luxury (LV/Rolex), everyday (Clorox/Domestos), single-idea (Volvo/Tesla/BMW), and Coca-Cola examples. Use it to coach brand owners toward a single, focused, feeling-led brand idea.

---

## Why Emotion Separates Strong Brands From Weak

A brand's true strength lies not just in the quality of its products but in the emotional connections it creates with customers. People don't simply buy products for their functionality — they buy them for **how they make them feel.** In most cases, purchasing decisions are driven by emotion first and justified with logic afterward.

**Luxury brands** aren't just selling superior craftsmanship — they are selling status, confidence, and a sense of accomplishment. A **Louis Vuitton** handbag or a **Rolex** watch is more than just an accessory; it's a symbol of success and admiration.

Even everyday products tap into emotional needs. Household cleaning brands like **Clorox** (USA) and **Domestos** (UK) don't just promise cleanliness; they offer protection and peace of mind, reinforcing the idea of a safe, healthy home for the family.

## Tell One Single, Focused Brand Idea

This emotional connection works across categories, from low-cost consumables to high-value purchases such as cars. What both have in common is that they tell stories with a **single, focused brand idea:**

- **Volvo** builds its identity around safety, portraying families confidently navigating the road, protected by advanced safety technology.
- **Tesla** emphasizes innovation and environmental consciousness, showcasing cutting-edge self-driving technology and a vision for a sustainable future.
- **BMW** sells the ultimate driving experience, capturing the thrill of performance, precision handling, and luxury.

These brands don't just sell products — they sell an identity and a lifestyle, making customers feel like they are part of something greater.

## The Psychology Behind It

Humans are emotional beings, and we make decisions based on how we feel — even for decisions that seem logical, like choosing laundry detergent. Our brains are wired to rely on emotions because emotions help us process information quickly and make decisions efficiently. When we encounter a brand, we unconsciously associate it with certain feelings drawn from past experiences, cultural influences, and our own desires and fears. If a brand can tap into positive emotions — happiness, security, or excitement — it's more likely to stick in our minds.

**Coca-Cola** is one of the most famous examples. For decades, it has positioned itself as more than a soft drink — it's associated with happiness, togetherness, and sharing special moments. Its iconic holiday campaigns reinforce this. Coca-Cola isn't selling a beverage; it's selling the feeling of joy and connection. Its famous **"Share a Coke"** campaign, which printed people's names on bottles, encouraged people to share meaningful moments with friends and family. By personalizing the product, Coca-Cola made it feel special and personal — driving a significant increase in sales and deepening loyalty.

## Coaching Application
Ask the brand owner to name the *one* feeling their brand owns — Volvo owns safety, BMW owns the drive. If they can't name it in a single word, the brand idea isn't focused enough. Then check whether every touchpoint reinforces that single feeling rather than listing features.
