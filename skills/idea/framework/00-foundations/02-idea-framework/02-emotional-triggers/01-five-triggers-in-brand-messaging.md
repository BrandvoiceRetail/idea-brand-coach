# SKILL — Five Emotional Triggers in Brand Messaging

## Purpose
Five high-leverage emotional triggers, each with a brand that has mastered it: FOMO (Supreme), Belonging (CrossFit), Empathy (Always), Nostalgia (Nintendo), Achievement/Status (Hermès). Where the table gives copy formulas, this gives deep case studies of triggers built into a whole brand. Use it to show what a single trigger looks like when it becomes a brand's identity.

---

## Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)
People are naturally afraid of being left behind or missing out on an opportunity. Brands can leverage this fear by creating a sense of urgency or exclusivity — limited-time offers, exclusive launches, or members-only deals.

**Example: Supreme**, the streetwear brand, has perfected the use of FOMO. Their limited-edition product drops — where items often sell out in minutes — create an intense sense of urgency. The brand's **"drop culture"** generates huge emotional demand, with fans lining up for hours or scouring resale markets. Supreme has cultivated a powerful brand around scarcity, making every purchase feel like an accomplishment and a status symbol.

## Belonging and Community
Humans have a deep-seated need to belong to a group or community. Brands that create a sense of belonging build strong, loyal followings by positioning a product as a gateway to a larger community.

**Example: CrossFit** has built a worldwide community of fitness enthusiasts. While the program is itself a product — an intense workout regimen — the brand has cultivated an identity and community around the experience. CrossFitters identify strongly with the brand because they feel connected to other members through shared goals, challenges, and achievements. This sense of belonging has turned CrossFit into more than a workout; it's a lifestyle customers are emotionally invested in.

## Empathy and Compassion
Brands that demonstrate empathy — showing they understand and care about their customers' struggles — forge deep emotional connections. When a brand shows compassion, customers feel it truly cares about them, not just their money.

**Example: Always**, the feminine hygiene brand, tackled the issue of girls' self-esteem with their **Like a Girl** campaign. The campaign challenged the harmful stereotypes around the phrase "like a girl," which had often been used as an insult. By showing empathy towards young girls struggling with self-confidence, Always connected emotionally with its audience and the broader public — reframing doing things "like a girl" as a strength, not a weakness. This strengthened Always's brand equity by aligning it with the empowerment of women and girls.

## Nostalgia
People often have strong emotional attachments to their past, and brands that tap into nostalgia create powerful connections by reminding people of happier times, childhood memories, or past experiences — evoking warmth, safety, and joy.

**Example: Nintendo** has successfully used nostalgia. Many of its customers grew up playing classic games like Super Mario or The Legend of Zelda, and by re-releasing older consoles or games in new formats (like the Nintendo Switch or the NES Classic Edition), the company taps into those fond childhood memories. This nostalgia drives emotional engagement, making fans excited to relive their past while introducing the brand to a new generation.

## Achievement and Status
Many consumers seek recognition and validation, and brands that position their products as symbols of achievement or status create strong emotional appeal.

**Example: Hermès**, the luxury brand, is known for its **Birkin** bag, one of the most exclusive and expensive handbags in the world. The Birkin has become a status symbol representing wealth, success, and high social standing. Owning one isn't just about having a high-quality product; it's about achieving something rare and desirable. By limiting production and creating waitlists, Hermès has built an air of exclusivity, making it a product that signifies achievement and status.

## Coaching Application
Each of these is a single trigger taken to its logical extreme until it becomes the brand. Ask the brand owner: if you could only own *one* trigger, which would it be — and are you willing to build the whole brand around it the way Supreme owns FOMO or Hermès owns status? Pick one, then make every touchpoint reinforce it.
