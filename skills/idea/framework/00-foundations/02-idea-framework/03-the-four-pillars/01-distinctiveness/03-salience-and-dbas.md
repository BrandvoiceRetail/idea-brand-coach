# SKILL — Salience and Distinctive Brand Assets

## Purpose
Brings difference and distinctiveness together into salience — how readily a brand comes to mind in buying situations — and introduces Distinctive Brand Assets (DBAs) and the "say less, more often" rule. Carries the KFC example. Use it as the synthesis: the strongest brands are both meaningfully unique and immediately recognizable.

---

## Combine Both, or Be Forgettable

> A brand can be **distinctive without being different**, but being **different without distinctiveness** makes it forgettable. The strongest brands combine both — ensuring they are meaningfully unique while also being immediately recognizable.

## Salience

**Salience** is the measure of how readily a brand comes to mind in buying situations. Building salience requires consistent, memorable, and widely distributed communication. The principle is simple: **say less, more often, across more channels.** This means focusing on a few meaningful brand attributes and repeating them until they become second nature to the consumer. Consistency across touchpoints is key, as fragmented messaging dilutes the brand's ability to create strong memory structures.

## Distinctive Brand Assets (DBA)

To achieve salience, brands need to leverage **Distinctive Brand Assets (DBA)** — the logos, colors, typography, taglines, and even sounds that make a brand unmistakably itself. These assets should not only be memorable but also intrinsically tied to the brand's identity.

For example, **KFC's** red-and-white buckets, Colonel Sanders iconography, and "finger-lickin' good" tagline have helped it dominate consumer recognition.

> Salience is the result of being consistently distinctive. Brands that master both differentiation and distinctiveness ensure that they are not just seen but also remembered and chosen.

## Coaching Application
The operating instruction is "say less, more often." When a brand owner wants to add a new message, push back — salience comes from repeating a *few* attributes everywhere, not from saying more things once. Inventory their DBAs (logo, colors, typography, taglines, sounds): are they consistent across every touchpoint, or fragmented? Consistency is what builds the memory structure that gets the brand chosen.
