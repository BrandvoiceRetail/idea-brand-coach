# SKILL — How to Be Authentic (Three Steps)

## Purpose
The three practical steps for putting the Authenticity pillar to work: define your core values, align your actions with them, and be transparent and honest. Carries the Ben & Jerry's example. Use it as the action checklist for closing the gap between what a brand says and what it does.

---

## Step 1: Define Your Core Values
What does your brand stand for? What are the values that drive your mission? Authentic brands have a clear set of values that guide not only their marketing efforts but their entire business model. Your values should be **more than just words** — they should reflect how you conduct business, treat customers, and produce your products.

**Example: Ben & Jerry's** has built its brand on a foundation of social justice and environmental activism. From fighting for climate change to advocating for marriage equality, Ben & Jerry's doesn't just make ice cream; it uses its platform to champion important social causes. This authenticity strengthens its connection with socially conscious consumers.

## Step 2: Align Actions with Values
Your brand's actions should consistently reflect your values. If your brand promises sustainability, you need to ensure your **supply chain, materials, and production processes** all align with that promise. Authenticity is built on consistency, and customers will quickly lose trust if they see a disconnect between your messaging and actions.

## Step 3: Be Transparent and Honest
Transparency is a critical component of authenticity. In today's digital world, customers expect brands to be open about how they operate, especially when it comes to sensitive issues like data privacy, sourcing, and environmental impact. **When you make mistakes, admit them. When you achieve milestones, celebrate them with your customers.**

## Coaching Application
Work the steps in order, but spend the most time on step 2 — it's where authenticity is won or lost. Defining values (step 1) is easy; aligning the supply chain and operations to them is the hard, credible part. For step 3, coach the brand owner that admitting mistakes openly builds more trust than a flawless facade — transparency, including about failures, is the proof of authenticity.
