# SKILL — Why Borrowed Trust Is a Risky Strategy

## Purpose
The three structural reasons depending on influencers as the *primary* trust tool is a critical mistake. Use this as a checklist to show a seller the hidden fragility of an influencer-led strategy.

---

## The Three Risks

### 1. Trust Can't Be Transferred
> "Trust doesn't work like a hand-me-down jacket — it doesn't automatically pass from an influencer to a brand."

Consumers might trust an influencer, but that trust doesn't extend to every product they promote. **The real relationship exists between the influencer and their audience — not between the audience and the brand.** When the partnership ends, so does the connection; if the brand hasn't established its own credibility, it becomes invisible once the influencer stops talking about it.

### 2. Lack of Control Over the Narrative
When brands hand storytelling to influencers, they lose control over how the message is delivered. Influencers have their own style, tone, and priorities, which may not align with the brand's core values. **Worse: if an influencer is caught in controversy, aligned brands suffer collateral damage.**

### 3. Short-Term Gains, Long-Term Dependence
Influencer marketing is great for short bursts of visibility but rarely translates into long-term loyalty. A brand that relies too heavily on influencers is always **at the mercy of who is talking about them**, rather than building a stable, independent reputation.

## The Cycle of Dependency
Relying on influencers creates a self-perpetuating trap:

> - No influencer promotion? Sales drop.
> - Want visibility? Pay an influencer again.
> - Repeat endlessly.

> "This isn't trust-building — it's a paid traffic model that forces brands to keep spending to stay relevant."

## Coaching Application
When a seller's growth stops the moment they stop paying for reach, name the cycle of dependency. The antidote is owned credibility — see [[04-four-ways-to-own-your-trust]].
