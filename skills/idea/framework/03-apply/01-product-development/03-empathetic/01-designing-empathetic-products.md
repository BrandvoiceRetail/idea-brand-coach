# SKILL — Designing Empathetic Products

## Purpose
The four tactics for building empathy into a product. Preserve the worked examples — Slack (solve real problems), Starbucks app (personalize), OXO Good Grips (accessibility), and Warby Parker (map the journey).

---

## The Tactics

### 1. Solve Real Customer Problems
The best products solve **genuine** customer problems. Use the insights gathered from Avatar 2.0 to identify pain points in your customers' lives and design products that offer solutions. Empathy means putting yourself in your customer's shoes and asking, **"What do they really need?"**
> Example: Slack (https://slack.com), the workplace messaging app, was developed to solve a common pain point in corporate environments: ineffective communication. By simplifying team collaboration and reducing email overload, Slack creates a more efficient and less stressful work environment. This empathetic design has made it indispensable for many businesses.

### 2. Offer Personalized Experiences
Empathetic products allow for **personalization**, recognizing that every customer is unique. Offering customizable options or tailored experiences makes customers feel valued and understood.
> Example: Starbucks' mobile app personalizes the customer experience by recommending drinks based on past orders, offering customized rewards, and allowing users to modify their beverages to their exact preferences. This tailored approach makes each interaction feel more personal and engaging.

### 3. Design for Accessibility
Empathy also means designing products that are **inclusive and accessible to all** — whether that's offering products for different body types, designing interfaces for users with disabilities, or using packaging that's easy to open. Empathetic brands consider the needs of every potential user.
> Example: OXO Good Grips are kitchen tools designed with empathy at their core. The brand's founder was inspired to create kitchen utensils that could be used comfortably by people with arthritis, leading to the development of ergonomic, easy-to-use products that cater to a wide range of abilities.

### 4. Map the Customer Journey
Empathy goes beyond the product itself — it's about understanding the **entire customer journey.** From product discovery to purchase and post-purchase experiences, every touchpoint should be designed with empathy to minimize friction and enhance satisfaction.
> Example: Warby Parker (www.warbyparker.com/) disrupted the eyewear industry by empathizing with customers who found traditional eyeglass shopping inconvenient and expensive. By offering free home try-on kits and affordable frames, Warby Parker created a customer journey that is convenient, risk-free, and centered around the customer's needs.

## Coaching Application
Work the four tactics as a checklist against a product: Does it solve a real pain (Slack)? Does it adapt to the individual (Starbucks)? Can everyone use it (OXO)? Is the whole journey — not just the product — frictionless (Warby Parker)? OXO is the reminder that accessibility is a design choice from the start, not an afterthought.
