# SKILL — Distinctive Product Design

## Purpose
The second IDEA pillar applied to product development: aim for salience, not genuine uniqueness. Preserve the Distinctive Brand Assets (DBAs) definition and the "say less, more often" principle.

---

## Salience Over Uniqueness
Creating a product that truly stands out is more important than ever, but **genuine uniqueness — a product that is truly different — is rare.** What we can aim for is **salience**: ensuring your product becomes the one that customers notice, remember, and choose.

Salience isn't just about being different; it's about being **distinctive in a way that captures attention and resonates with your audience.** Distinctiveness drives salience by making your brand and product memorable, even in categories that might seem commoditized or generic — think bottled water.

## Positioning Is a Means, Not the Goal
Move beyond simply creating a product and focus on **how it is perceived.** Positioning isn't the end goal — it's a **means** to achieve differentiation and distinction. The key is to craft brand and product attributes that are not only distinct but also **meaningful** to your target audience, creating both an emotional and functional connection that sets you apart.

Building a distinctive product starts with insights. Using tools like Avatar 2.0, we deeply understand our audience and identify what they value most, then design with salience in mind — whether through innovation, functionality, or an emotional experience customers can't ignore.

## Distinctive Brand Assets (DBAs)
To reinforce distinctiveness, **Distinctive Brand Assets (DBAs)** play a crucial role. These elements — **like logos, packaging, colors, or taglines** — ensure that your product and brand consistently "look like themselves" across all touchpoints.

## Say Less, More Often
There is power in **saying less but saying it more often, across more channels**, to amplify your brand's impact.

## Coaching Application
Don't chase a product that is objectively unique — chase one that is noticed, remembered, and chosen. Audit the brand's DBAs (logo, packaging, colors, taglines) for consistency across every touchpoint; an asset that changes from channel to channel can't build mental availability. Then resist message sprawl: pick the fewest distinctive ideas and repeat them more often.
