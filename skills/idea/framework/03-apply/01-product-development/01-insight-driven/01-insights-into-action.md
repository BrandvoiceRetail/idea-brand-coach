# SKILL — Turning Insights into Action

## Purpose
The three-step process for converting Avatar 2.0 insights into a built product. Preserve the worked examples — sustainability avatar, Blue Apron/HelloFresh, and Dyson — and the closing principle that people buy on feeling, not function.

---

## The Process
Using insight-driven principles, here's how to apply what you've learned from the Avatar 2.0 tool to your product development process.

### 1. Identify Key Emotional Triggers
Start by mapping out the emotional triggers identified through Avatar 2.0. **What emotions drive your customers' purchasing decisions?** Do they seek products that offer convenience, comfort, confidence, or social status?
> Example: If your customer avatar highlights that your audience is concerned with environmental sustainability, your product should reflect eco-consciousness through sustainable materials, ethical sourcing, and environmentally friendly packaging.

### 2. Translate Insights into Features
Use customer insights to inform the features you build into your product. If your customers are motivated by efficiency and convenience, then developing time-saving features or streamlined user interfaces should be a top priority.
> Example: The rise of meal-kit companies like Blue Apron (www.blueapron.com) and HelloFresh (www.hellofresh.co.uk) is a direct result of understanding that busy consumers value convenience but also want healthier, home-cooked meals. By offering pre-portioned ingredients and easy-to-follow recipes, these companies deliver a product that aligns with the emotional needs of their audience — saving time while promoting health and family bonding.

### 3. Focus on Solving Emotional Needs
Remember, **people don't buy products solely for their functional benefits — they buy based on how a product makes them feel.** Your product should address emotional needs like security, comfort, pride, or belonging. This creates an emotional bond with the customer and elevates your product beyond the competition.
> Example: Dyson (www.dyson.com), best known for its vacuum cleaners and hair dryers, doesn't just sell functionality. Dyson's products are marketed as innovative, premium tools that offer consumers a sense of pride in ownership and a feeling of luxury. The product design, engineering, and marketing all emphasize high-performance innovation, positioning Dyson as more than just a home appliance — it's a status symbol.

## Coaching Application
Run a product through the three steps in order: name the trigger (step 1), translate it into a concrete feature (step 2), then check that the feature lands on the underlying feeling, not just the function (step 3). The Dyson test catches the common failure — a brand that lists specs but never names the emotion (pride, luxury) those specs deliver.
