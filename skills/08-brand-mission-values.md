# SKILL 08 — Brand Mission & Brand Values

## Purpose
This skill powers the Brand Mission and Brand Values outputs of the IDEA Brand Canvas. These two elements translate the brand's purpose and vision into concrete principles and actions. The coach must be able to distinguish between the four brand statements and help users create mission and values that are genuinely actionable, not decorative.

---

## Brand Mission: How We Serve

The brand mission defines the actionable steps taken to fulfil the brand's purpose and vision. It reflects HOW the brand serves its customers while maintaining authenticity and trust.

Where purpose is the "why" and vision is the "where we are going," mission is the "what we do and how we do it." It should be practical, specific, and achievable — not aspirational.

---

## The Four IDEA Tests for Brand Mission

INSIGHT-DRIVEN: Does this mission address specific customer challenges? Is it grounded in real understanding of what customers need?

DISTINCTIVE: Does this mission define a unique way of delivering value? Is this how this brand specifically serves customers — not how any brand could?

EMPATHETIC: Does this mission focus on improving customers' lives — not just the brand's commercial goals?

AUTHENTIC: Does this mission ensure consistency in messaging, experience, and values? Are the actions it describes actually happening in the business?

---

## Anatomy of a Strong Mission Statement

A mission statement should:
1. Define what the brand does
2. Define who it does it for
3. Define how it does it (the distinctive method or approach)
4. Connect to a meaningful outcome for the customer

WEAK: "To deliver high-quality products with excellent service." (Generic, says nothing specific about approach or customer outcome.)

STRONG: "To design innovative, eco-friendly solutions that help individuals and families reduce their environmental footprint without compromising quality or convenience." (Specific product approach, specific customer, specific outcome, specific value.)

---

## Mission vs Purpose: The Key Difference

PURPOSE = Why we exist ("To empower eco-conscious consumers to live more sustainably")
MISSION = How we fulfil that purpose every day ("To design innovative, eco-friendly solutions that make sustainable choices accessible and convenient")

The mission is the operational expression of the purpose. If the purpose says "we exist to build trust," the mission says "we do that by being radically transparent about our ingredients, pricing, and processes."

---

## AI Prompt Template for Brand Mission

"Craft a clear and actionable brand mission statement that details how this company serves its customers while maintaining authenticity and trust. Ensure it highlights the unique value the brand delivers and how it aligns with customers' needs and aspirations, using the IDEA framework. The brand's purpose is [purpose statement]. It serves [customer avatar from Avatar 2.0]. Its primary emotional trigger is [trigger]."

## Brand Values: The Guiding Principles

Brand values are the foundational principles that shape how a brand interacts with customers, employees, and stakeholders. They create a foundation for consistency, trust, and emotional connection.

Values are not aspirational goals — they are descriptions of who the brand already is and how it already behaves. If a brand claims "integrity" as a value but hides its ingredient sourcing, that is not a value — it is a liability.

---

## The Four IDEA Tests for Brand Values

INSIGHT-DRIVEN: Do these values reflect what customers actually value in a brand beyond products? Are they grounded in real customer insight?

DISTINCTIVE: Do these values set the brand apart through a strong moral or ethical standpoint? Are they specific enough to be ownable?

EMPATHETIC: Do these values align with customer beliefs, fostering loyalty and advocacy?

AUTHENTIC: Are the brand's actions consistently reflecting these values? Is there genuine alignment between what the brand says and does?

---

## Anatomy of Strong Brand Values

Strong brand values should be:
- Specific, not generic (not "quality" and "innovation" — every brand claims these)
- Behavioural — they describe HOW the brand acts, not WHAT it believes in the abstract
- Demonstrable — there should be real examples of the values in action
- Customer-relevant — they should connect to what the target audience actually cares about
- Limited — three to five values that are genuinely held are more powerful than ten that are performative

WEAK: "Quality, Innovation, Excellence, Integrity, Customer Focus." (Generic, indistinguishable from any other brand.)

STRONG: "Radical transparency in everything we do. Craftsmanship that lasts decades. Environmental responsibility without compromise. Community above profit." (Each one is specific, demonstrable, and ownable.)

---

## Values in Action: Real Examples

BEN & JERRY'S: Built their brand on social justice and environmental activism. They actively campaign, speak out, and put their ice cream sales to work for causes they believe in. Their values are visible in every business decision.

PATAGONIA: "We're in business to save our home planet." They donate 1% of sales to environmental causes. They have a programme repairing old gear to reduce consumption. Their values cost them money — and that is precisely what makes them credible.

EVERLANE: "Radical transparency." They publish the true cost of making every garment, including materials, labour, and transport. The value is specific, actionable, and differentiating.

---

## Common Values Mistakes

- Listing values that look good but are never demonstrated operationally
- Choosing the same values as every competitor (every e-commerce brand claims "quality" and "customer satisfaction")
- Values that are about the brand, not about the customer or the wider world
- Too many values — creates the impression that the brand has not thought carefully about what actually matters

## AI Prompt Template for Brand Values

"Identify the core values that define this brand's character and ethical principles. Ensure these values reflect customer expectations, create an emotional connection, and set the brand apart in its category. Use the IDEA framework to make them distinctive, empathetic, and authentic. Provide examples of how these values should be consistently demonstrated in branding, messaging, and operations. The brand is [description], its purpose is [purpose], its core customer is [avatar]."

---

## Coaching Application

When a user cannot articulate their brand values, ask:
1. "What would you refuse to do for more sales — what line would you never cross?"
2. "What do your best customers say they love about you beyond the product itself?"
3. "What decisions in your business are non-negotiable, regardless of commercial pressure?"
4. "If your brand had a personality, what would it refuse to compromise on?"

When evaluating draft values, challenge any that are generic, undemonstrable, or disconnected from the customer experience. Ask: "Can you give me a specific example of this value in action in your business right now?"
